Overview

Financial ratios are the shortcuts professional analysts use to quickly assess a company's valuation, profitability, and financial health. Master these four critical ratios and you'll be able to evaluate any stock in minutes instead of hours.

Time to complete: 25-30 minutes

Prerequisites

  • Completion of Lesson 7.2: Reading Financial Statements
  • Understanding of earnings per share (EPS)
  • Basic division and percentage calculations

Core Content

What are Financial Ratios?

Financial Ratios are calculations that compare different financial metrics to reveal insights about valuation, profitability, and financial health.

Key Principle: A ratio by itself is meaningless. Compare it to:

  • Historical average (is it higher or lower than usual?)
  • Industry average (how does it compare to competitors?)
  • Market average (is it expensive or cheap vs. market?)

Ratio 1: P/E Ratio (Price-to-Earnings)

What It Shows: How many dollars investors pay for $1 of annual profit.

Formula:


P/E = Stock Price √∑ Earnings Per Share



Example:

Stock Price: $100

EPS: $5

P/E = 100 √∑ 5 = 20



Interpretation: Paying $20 for every $1 of profit

Real Examples:


Apple: Stock $180, EPS $6, P/E = 30

- Investors pay $30 for every $1 of profit

- Vs. S&P 500 average P/E of 20

- Apple is 50% more expensive than average

- Why? Because Apple is high-quality company with strong competitive advantage



General Motors: Stock $40, EPS $8, P/E = 5

- Investors pay $5 for every $1 of profit

- Vs. S&P 500 average P/E of 20

- GM is 75% cheaper than average

- Why? Because GM is mature, slow-growth, cyclical business

How to Interpret P/E:

| P/E Level | Interpretation | Risk |

|---|---|---|

| 0-10 | Very cheap (or company losing money) | Value trap risk |

| 10-15 | Cheap | Low downside risk |

| 15-25 | Fair value | Moderate |

| 25-40 | Expensive | Higher downside risk |

| 40+ | Very expensive | High downside risk |

P/E Considerations:

Trailing P/E: Uses past 12 months of earnings

  • More accurate (real earnings)
  • Less forward-looking

Forward P/E: Uses estimated next 12 months of earnings

  • Based on analyst forecasts
  • More growth-oriented
  • Can be wrong if estimates miss

Real Example: Growth Stock Premium


Company A (Mature):

- Current Earnings: $10

- Expected Future Earnings: $10.50 (5% growth)

- P/E: 20 (investors not paying much premium for slow growth)



Company B (Growth):

- Current Earnings: $1

- Expected Future Earnings: $2 (100% growth)

- P/E: 50 (investors pay premium for fast growth)



But Company B is riskier (growth might not materialize)

Company A is safer (stable but slow)

Both valuations can be appropriate depending on execution risk

Ratio 2: P/B Ratio (Price-to-Book)

What It Shows: How many dollars investors pay for $1 of book value (assets minus liabilities per share).

Formula:


P/B = Stock Price √∑ Book Value Per Share



Example:

Stock Price: $100

Book Value Per Share: $50

P/B = 100 √∑ 50 = 2.0



Interpretation: Paying 2x the book value

How to Interpret P/B:

| P/B Level | Meaning |

|---|---|

| P/B < 1.0 | Stock trading below asset value (possible bargain or trouble sign) |

| P/B = 1.0-2.0 | Fair value |

| P/B = 2.0-5.0 | Expensive (especially for low-growth companies) |

| P/B > 5.0 | Very expensive (better for high-growth or high-return companies) |

Real Example: Asset-Heavy vs. Asset-Light


Bank:

- Stock Price: $100

- Book Value: $95 (lots of assets like loans, buildings)

- P/B: 1.05

- Fair for banking because value is in assets



Tech Software Company:

- Stock Price: $100

- Book Value: $10 (fewer physical assets, mostly people/IP)

- P/B: 10

- Fair for tech because value is in intellectual property, not assets



Same P/B ratio looks expensive for banks, cheap for tech companies

Context matters!

Ratio 3: ROE (Return on Equity)

What It Shows: How much profit the company generates from shareholder equity (money invested).

Formula:


ROE = Net Income √∑ Shareholders' Equity



Example:

Net Income: $50M

Shareholders' Equity: $500M

ROE = 50 √∑ 500 = 10% (or 0.10)



Interpretation: Company generates 10% return on shareholder capital

How to Interpret ROE:

| ROE Level | Quality |

|---|---|

| ROE < 10% | Poor (money would grow faster in savings account) |

| ROE 10-15% | Average |

| ROE 15-20% | Good |

| ROE 20%+ | Excellent (great business model) |

Real Example: ROE Comparison


Company A:

- Net Income: $100M

- Shareholders' Equity: $1000M

- ROE = 10%

- Interpretation: Average business, not great returns



Company B:

- Net Income: $100M

- Shareholders' Equity: $500M

- ROE = 20%

- Interpretation: Excellent business, generating strong returns



Company B is better even though both have same profit

Because it's doing it with less shareholder capital (more efficient)

Historical ROE Leaders:


Apple: ROE ~80% (exceptional - uses debt, strong profits)

Microsoft: ROE ~30% (excellent)

Amazon: ROE ~15% (good but reinvesting for growth)

Bank of America: ROE ~10% (average for banks)

Ratio 4: Debt-to-Equity

What It Shows: How much debt the company is using compared to equity (financial leverage).

Formula:


Debt-to-Equity = Total Debt √∑ Shareholders' Equity



Example:

Total Debt: $200M

Shareholders' Equity: $500M

D/E = 200 √∑ 500 = 0.4



Interpretation: For every $1 of equity, company has $0.40 of debt

How to Interpret D/E:

| D/E Ratio | Risk Level | Implication |

|---|---|---|

| D/E < 0.5 | Very safe | Conservative capital structure |

| D/E 0.5-1.0 | Safe | Moderate leverage |

| D/E 1.0-2.0 | Moderate Risk | Higher risk in downturns |

| D/E > 2.0 | High Risk | Vulnerable to economic stress |

Real Example: Debt Varies by Industry


Tech Company:

- Debt: $1B

- Equity: $100B

- D/E: 0.01 (virtually no debt, very safe)



Bank:

- Debt: $800B

- Equity: $100B

- D/E: 8.0 (very high, but normal for banks)



Utility:

- Debt: $40B

- Equity: $60B

- D/E: 0.67 (moderate, typical for utilities)



Same D/E ratio means different things in different industries

Tech with high D/E = DANGEROUS

Banks with high D/E = NORMAL

Why D/E Matters:


During Economic Boom:

- Low D/E: Stable, less profit

- High D/E: Higher profits (using leverage), riskier



During Recession:

- Low D/E: Can weather the storm

- High D/E: Debt payments become burden, risk of bankruptcy

Combining the Four Ratios

Complete Valuation and Quality Picture:


VALUATION (Is the stock cheap or expensive?)

‚Üí P/E Ratio (price relative to profit)

‚Üí P/B Ratio (price relative to assets)



QUALITY (Is this a good business?)

‚Üí ROE (how much profit on shareholder capital)



FINANCIAL STRENGTH (Can they survive downturns?)

‚Üí Debt-to-Equity (leverage risk)

Real Example: Complete Analysis


Stock A:

- P/E: 15 (cheap relative to market)

- P/B: 1.2 (fair value)

- ROE: 10% (average quality)

- D/E: 0.3 (very safe)

Interpretation: Cheap stock, average quality, safe

Good for: Conservative investors wanting value



Stock B:

- P/E: 35 (expensive relative to market)

- P/B: 4.0 (expensive)

- ROE: 25% (excellent quality)

- D/E: 0.5 (moderate leverage)

Interpretation: Expensive stock, excellent quality, safe

Good for: Growth investors willing to pay premium for quality



Stock C:

- P/E: 8 (very cheap)

- P/B: 0.5 (trading below book value)

- ROE: 3% (terrible quality)

- D/E: 2.5 (high debt, dangerous)

Interpretation: Cheap for a reason, poor business, risky

Danger: Value trap, potential bankruptcy

Industry Variations

Ratios vary significantly by industry:


Banks (Citigroup):

- High D/E (8.0+) = NORMAL

- Moderate P/E (8-12) = TYPICAL

- Lower ROE (8-12%) = TYPICAL



Tech (Apple):

- Low D/E (0.5) = NORMAL

- Higher P/E (25-35) = TYPICAL

- Very High ROE (50%+) = TYPICAL



Utilities (Duke Energy):

- Moderate D/E (0.8) = NORMAL

- Lower P/E (12-15) = TYPICAL

- Moderate ROE (10-12%) = TYPICAL



Never compare ratios across industries directly

Always compare within industry

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Mistake #1: Using P/E Alone

Problem: Stock has low P/E so you buy. Later you learn company is in decline.

Solution: Combine P/E with ROE and growth rate. Low P/E only good if company is profitable and growing.

Mistake #2: Ignoring the Industry Context

Problem: Tech stock has D/E of 1.5 which looks risky. But that's normal for tech.

Solution: Always compare ratios to industry averages, not across industries.

Mistake #3: Using Stale Data

Problem: You use P/E from quarterly earnings, stock has rallied 20% since then.

Solution: Update your data before making decisions. Markets move fast.

Mistake #4: Confusing High P/E with Expensive

Problem: Stock has P/E of 40, so it's overpriced. But if it's growing 50%/year, maybe justified.

Solution: Consider growth rate. PEG ratio = P/E √∑ growth rate. Compare to 1.0 as benchmark.

Mistake #5: Not Checking if Earnings Are Real

Problem: Low P/E ratio looks great. But company has negative cash flow.

Solution: Always verify earnings with cash flow statement. Earnings can be accounting fiction.

Real-World Application

Using Ratios in Practice

Scenario: Picking Between Two Dividend Stocks


Stock A (Old Utility):

- P/E: 12

- P/B: 1.0

- ROE: 8%

- D/E: 0.7

- Dividend: 4%

Appeal: Very cheap, stable



Stock B (Growing Tech):

- P/E: 30

- P/B: 3.5

- ROE: 22%

- D/E: 0.3

- Dividend: 1%

Appeal: Quality, growing



Decision:

- If you want income: Choose A (4% dividend)

- If you want growth: Choose B (higher ROE, lower D/E safer)

- Risk tolerance: A safer (lower D/E), B riskier (growth dependent)

Both decisions defensible, depends on your needs

Practice Exercise

Exercise 1: Calculate Ratios

Given this data for Company XYZ:

  • Stock Price: $50
  • EPS: $2.50
  • Book Value Per Share: $25
  • Net Income: $500M
  • Shareholders' Equity: $5,000M
  • Total Debt: $2,500M

Calculate:

  1. P/E Ratio: _
  2. P/B Ratio: _
  3. ROE: _
  4. Debt-to-Equity: _

Answer Key:

  1. P/E = 50 √∑ 2.50 = 20
  2. P/B = 50 √∑ 25 = 2.0
  3. ROE = 500 √∑ 5000 = 10%
  4. D/E = 2500 √∑ 5000 = 0.5

Exercise 2: Interpret Ratios

For your calculated ratios above, write brief interpretation:

  1. Is the stock expensive or cheap? (Compare P/E of 20 to market average of 18)
  2. Is the book value reasonable? (Is P/B of 2.0 fair?)
  3. Is the ROE good? (10% is average or excellent?)
  4. Is the debt reasonable? (Is D/E of 0.5 safe?)

Exercise 3: Compare Companies

Pick two companies in same industry (Apple vs. Microsoft, or Nike vs. Adidas):

  1. Get their most recent P/E, P/B, ROE, and D/E
  2. Compare which is more expensive
  3. Compare which has better quality (ROE)
  4. Compare which is safer (D/E)
  5. Which would you buy and why?

Key Takeaways

‚úì P/E Ratio = Valuation metric (cheap/expensive)

‚úì P/B Ratio = Asset valuation (works better for asset-heavy companies)

‚úì ROE = Quality metric (how efficiently company uses capital)

‚úì Debt-to-Equity = Risk metric (financial leverage and safety)

‚úì Compare to industry = Never compare ratios across different industries

‚úì Historical trends = Better to look at 5-year average vs. single number

‚úì All four matter = Use all four for complete picture
