Lesson 14.3: Building Your Trading Business Plan

Overview

Most traders are hobbyists with no plan. Professional traders have documented businesses. A business plan transforms trading from gambling into an enterprise with defined objectives, strategies, and metrics. It's the difference between "I hope I make money" and "Here's exactly how I'll make money."

Why You Need a Business Plan

The Difference

Without a Plan (Hobbyist):


"I'm going to make money trading stocks"

- No defined strategy

- Trades based on gut feeling

- No risk management

- Returns vary wildly (+50% one month, -30% next)

- Frequently changes approach

- No documentation

- Average result: Break-even or slight loss

- Tax complications (is this business or hobby?)

With a Plan (Professional):


"I execute 15-25 swing trades monthly, targeting 2-3% per trade,

risking 1% per trade, holding 3-10 days, using price action +

volume analysis. Expected return: 10-15%/year. I track every trade,

measure Sharpe ratio, and adjust only when tested system fails."



- Defined, testable strategy

- Consistent entry/exit rules

- Strict risk management

- Predictable performance

- Evolution based on data, not emotion

- Full documentation

- Professional tax treatment

- Average result: Consistent profitability

Operations and Risk Management (2 pages)

Risk Management Rules:


MY NON-NEGOTIABLE RULES:



Rule #1: 1% Risk Per Trade

Never risk more than 1% of account on single trade

Example: $20,000 account = Max $200 at risk per trade

Status: ‚òê Always followed  ‚òê Occasionally bend  ‚òê Often violated

Action: Must tighten discipline immediately



Rule #2: Maximum Loss Per Day

Will not accept losses exceeding ____% in single day

Example: 2% daily max = $400 max loss on $20,000

Example: Lose $300 AM trade, max $100 more PM trades

Status: ‚òê Strict  ‚òê Flexible  ‚òê Non-existent

Action: Close trading for the day once hit



Rule #3: Monthly Loss Limit

Will not exceed ____% monthly loss

Example: 5% monthly max = $1,000 on $20,000

If exceeded: Halt trading, review system, reassess



Rule #4: No Revenge Trading

If I'm down, I do NOT increase position size or risk

Response to loss: Take a break, walk away, recalibrate

NOT: Double down to "make it back"



Rule #5: No Trading on Emotion

After major win: No increased risk-taking

After major loss: No revenge trades

After bad news: No panic selling without plan

Implementation: Pre-planned trades only, no reactive trades



Rule #6: Position Limit

Maximum # of concurrent positions: _____

Reason: Prevents over-concentration

Example: Never more than 5 open trades at once



Rule #7: Drawdown Limit

If cumulative loss reaches ____% of account:

Action: Stop trading, investigate system, don't restart until confident



These rules are NON-NEGOTIABLE. Period.

Business Structure Decision

Sole Proprietor vs. LLC vs. S-Corp


SOLE PROPRIETOR:

Structure: Your personal business (simplest)

Tax treatment: Personal tax return (Schedule C)

Self-employment tax: Full 15.3%

Liability: Personal‚Äîall business liability is your liability

Cost: Minimal ($0-100)

Good for: Part-time traders, low income ($30k-50k)

Tax paid: Annual



LLC (Limited Liability Company):

Structure: Business entity separate from you

Tax treatment: Pass-through (Schedule C or corporate)

Self-employment tax: Can be reduced with S-election

Liability: Protected (business issues don't become personal)

Cost: Setup $500-2,000, annual $300-1,000

Good for: Full-time traders, growing business

Tax advantage: Slight over sole proprietor



S-CORP:

Structure: Corporation with special tax election

Tax treatment: Corporate with S-election

Self-employment tax: Can be significantly reduced

  (Take salary: $50,000 + distributions $30,000)

  (Only salary subjects to SE tax, not all distributions)

Liability: Maximum protection

Cost: Setup $1,500-3,000, annual filing $500-2,000, accounting $2,000-4,000

Good for: Successful full-time traders earning $100k+

Tax savings: Can be $5,000-15,000+/year



DECISION FRAMEWORK:



Annual trading income < $50,000:

‚Üí Sole proprietor (simplest, lowest cost)



Annual trading income $50,000-100,000:

‚Üí LLC (some liability protection, minimal complexity)



Annual trading income > $100,000:

‚Üí S-Corp (tax savings exceed accounting costs)



If you're unsure: Start with sole proprietor, migrate to LLC as income grows

Key Takeaways

  1. A business plan transforms gambling into business - Gives structure and accountability
  2. Written strategy prevents emotional trading - You follow the plan, not your gut
  3. Documented goals enable measurement - You know exactly what success looks like
  4. Risk management rules must be non-negotiable - Or you'll break them under pressure
  5. Business structure impacts taxes significantly - Consider S-Corp if income >$100k
  6. Annual reviews enable continuous improvement - Data-driven evolution beats random changes
  7. Professional trader status requires documentation - Proves this is legitimate business
  8. Time commitment must be realistic - Know how many hours you can actually invest
