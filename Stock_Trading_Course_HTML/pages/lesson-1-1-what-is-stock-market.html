Prerequisites

  • Basic reading comprehension

Core Content

What is the Stock Market?

Imagine you have a great idea for a coffee shop. You've perfected your recipes, found the perfect location, and created a solid business plan. There's just one problem: you need $100,000 to get started, but you only have $20,000 in savings.

You have a few options:

  1. Take out a loan (and pay interest)
  2. Find investors who believe in your vision
  3. Wait years to save enough money

Now imagine instead you could invite 100 people to each invest $1,000 in your coffee shop. In return, they each own a small piece of your business. When your shop makes profit, they share in that success. If your shop grows to $1 million in value, their $1,000 investment is now worth $10,000.

This is the essence of the stock market.

The Simple Definition

Stock Market: A marketplace where shares (pieces of ownership) in companies are bought and sold.

Think of it like:

  • eBay for company ownership pieces
  • A farmers market but for investments instead of produce
  • Amazon but trading company shares instead of products

Why Does the Stock Market Exist?

The stock market serves two main purposes:

#### 1. Helps Companies Grow

Companies need money (called "capital") to:

  • Build factories
  • Hire employees
  • Develop new products
  • Expand to new locations
  • Fund research and development

Instead of borrowing money or waiting years to build profits, companies can sell shares of ownership to investors. This gives them immediate access to capital.

Real Example:

When Amazon started, Jeff Bezos had a garage and an idea. To grow into the global giant it is today, Amazon needed billions of dollars. By selling shares in the stock market, Amazon raised money from millions of investors worldwide.

#### 2. Helps People Build Wealth

The stock market allows everyday people to:

  • Own pieces of successful companies
  • Share in company profits
  • Build wealth over time
  • Save for retirement
  • Generate passive income

Real Example:

If you bought $1,000 of Apple stock in 2010, it would be worth over $10,000 today. You didn't work at Apple, build any iPhones, or run the company - but you shared in Apple's success by owning a small piece of it.

How Does It Work? (The Basic Mechanics)

Let's use a simple story:

Meet Sarah

Sarah owns a successful bakery. She wants to open 10 more locations but needs $500,000.

Going Public

Sarah decides to "take her company public" by offering shares on the stock market. She divides her company into 100,000 shares and sells 50,000 shares at $10 each.

The Math:

  • 50,000 shares √ó $10 = $500,000 (the capital she needs)
  • Sarah keeps 50,000 shares (50% ownership)
  • Public investors now own 50,000 shares (50% ownership)

Trading Begins

Now those 50,000 public shares can be bought and sold between investors. If Sarah's bakery succeeds and grows, each share might become worth $20, $30, or more. If it struggles, shares might fall to $8, $5, or less.

The Key Point:

Once shares are public, their price changes based on:

  • How well the company performs
  • What investors think the company is worth
  • Supply and demand (how many people want to buy vs sell)

Key Terminology

Stock: A piece of ownership in a company

  • Also called "share" or "equity"
  • Represents partial ownership

Share: One unit of stock

  • If you own 10 shares of Apple, you own 10 pieces of Apple

Shareholder: Someone who owns shares

  • Also called "stockholder" or "investor"
  • You become part-owner of the company

Public Company: A company whose shares trade on the stock market

  • Anyone can buy shares
  • Must follow strict reporting rules
  • Examples: Apple, Microsoft, Tesla

Private Company: A company not traded publicly

  • Shares not available to general public
  • No stock market trading
  • Examples: Cargill, Mars Inc., many startups

The Two Markets

Primary Market: Where companies first sell shares

  • Company raises money directly
  • Called an "IPO" (Initial Public Offering)
  • Happens once when company "goes public"

Secondary Market: Where investors trade among themselves

  • Most daily trading happens here
  • Company doesn't get money from these trades
  • This is what people mean by "the stock market"

Analogy:

  • Primary Market = Buying a car new from the dealer
  • Secondary Market = Buying/selling used cars between people

Why This Matters to You

Understanding the stock market's purpose helps you realize:

  1. You Can Participate: You don't need millions - you can buy single shares of most companies
  1. You Share in Real Business: Stocks aren't lottery tickets; they represent real companies with real products and profits
  1. Long-Term Wealth Building: Historically, the stock market has been one of the best ways to build wealth over time
  1. Risk and Reward: Because you own part of a business, you share both success (profits) and struggles (losses)

Real-World Application

How This Applies Today

Every day, millions of people trade stocks:

  • Retirement accounts invest in stock markets
  • Parents buy stocks for their children's education
  • Individuals build wealth through stock ownership

Real Companies You Know:

All of these are publicly traded (you can own pieces):

  • Apple (AAPL) - makes iPhones, iPads, MacBooks
  • Nike (NKE) - athletic shoes and apparel
  • Disney (DIS) - movies, theme parks, streaming
  • Starbucks (SBUX) - coffee shops worldwide

When you buy a share of Starbucks, you literally own a tiny piece of every Starbucks store, all their coffee inventory, their brand, and their future profits.

Why Companies Choose to Go Public

Advantages for Companies:

  • Raise large amounts of capital quickly
  • Don't have to pay back the money (unlike loans)
  • Increase company visibility and prestige
  • Create liquidity for early investors and employees

Disadvantages for Companies:

  • Must share profits with shareholders
  • Must follow strict reporting requirements
  • Public scrutiny and pressure
  • Founders give up some control

This is why some successful companies (like Chick-fil-A) choose to stay private.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Mistake #1: Thinking the Stock Market is Gambling

Reality: When you buy stock, you own part of a real business. With proper research and strategy, it's investing, not gambling.

Mistake #2: Believing You Need to Be Rich to Invest

Reality: You can buy single shares of most companies. Some stocks cost less than $10 per share. Many brokers now allow fractional shares (buying half or even a tenth of a share).

Mistake #3: Thinking Stock Market = Get Rich Quick

Reality: The stock market is a tool for building wealth over time. Quick riches are rare and usually involve high risk. Most successful investors build wealth slowly over decades.

Mistake #4: Being Scared to Learn

Reality: The stock market can seem complex, but the basics are simple. Every expert started as a beginner, just like you.

Quick Review

Let's recap the key takeaways:

What is the Stock Market?

  • A marketplace for buying and selling company ownership pieces (shares)

Why Does It Exist?

  • Helps companies raise money to grow
  • Helps people build wealth by owning profitable businesses

Key Terms:

  • Stock/Share: Piece of company ownership
  • Shareholder: Person who owns shares
  • Public Company: Company trading on stock market
  • Primary Market: Where companies first sell shares
  • Secondary Market: Where investors trade shares with each other

Main Principle:

When you buy stock, you become a part-owner of that company and share in its success (and risks).
