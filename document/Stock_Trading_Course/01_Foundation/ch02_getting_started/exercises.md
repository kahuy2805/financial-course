# Chapter 2 Exercises: Getting Started as a Trader

[← Back to Chapter 2](README.md) | [↑ Back to Course Index](../../MASTER_INDEX.md)

---

## Overview

Congratulations! You've completed all 6 lessons in Chapter 2. Now it's time to practice and reinforce your learning through hands-on exercises. These exercises are designed to help you:

- Apply your knowledge in real situations
- Build confidence before trading real money
- Identify gaps in your understanding
- Develop trading habits

**Time to complete:** 2-3 hours (can be spread over several sessions)

---

## How to Use These Exercises

1. **Read the lesson first** - Before attempting exercises, review the corresponding lesson
2. **Do them in order** - Exercises build on each other
3. **Be honest** - Don't skip exercises even if they seem hard
4. **Document everything** - Keep a trading journal of your answers
5. **Reflect** - After each section, think about what you learned

## Exercise Section 1: Broker Research and Selection

### Exercise 1.1: Broker Comparison

**Objective:** Compare at least 3 brokers to understand their differences

**Instructions:**
1. Visit the websites of these brokers:
   - Fidelity.com
   - Schwab.com
   - Webull.com (or E*TRADE.com)
   - One more broker of your choice

2. For each broker, research and fill in:
   - Stock commission per trade
   - Minimum account balance
   - Account types available
   - Mobile app rating (check app store)
   - Available research tools
   - Customer support options
   - Fractional share support?

3. Create a comparison table in your notes

4. Answer these questions:
   - Which broker has the lowest minimum?
   - Which has the best mobile app?
   - Which offers the most research tools?
   - Which would you choose as a beginner? Why?

**Grading:** Complete if you have full information on at least 3 brokers

---

### Exercise 1.2: Broker Platform Review

**Objective:** Test-drive a broker's platform before funding it

**Instructions:**
1. Open a FREE practice account (no money needed) at your chosen broker
2. Most brokers allow account opening without depositing
3. Explore the platform and answer:
   - How easy was the sign-up process? (1-5)
   - What's the most confusing part of the platform?
   - Can you easily find a quote for Apple (AAPL)?
   - Can you access research tools and news?
   - Is the mobile app easier or harder than website?
   - Would a beginner find this intuitive? (1-5)

4. Take screenshots of:
   - The home page
   - The research page
   - The trading screen
   - The quote for one stock

5. Write a 1-paragraph review of the platform

**Grading:** Complete if you've explored the platform and written a review

---

### Exercise 1.3: Broker Selection Final Decision

**Objective:** Make a final decision on which broker to use

**Instructions:**
1. Based on your research above, decide which broker you'll use
2. Write down your choice and explain:
   - Why you chose this broker
   - What features were most important?
   - What concerns do you still have?
   - When will you fund the account?

3. Join the broker's community/forums to see what other users say
   - Check Reddit r/stocks or r/investing
   - Look at Trustpilot reviews
   - Read recent news about the broker

4. Final decision: Is this still your choice after seeing user feedback?

**Grading:** Complete if you've made a final decision and explained your reasoning

## Exercise Section 2: Account Types

### Exercise 2.1: Account Type Assessment

**Objective:** Determine which accounts are right for YOUR situation

**Instructions:**
1. Answer these questions about yourself:
   - What's your age?
   - Do you have earned income (job/side gig)?
   - How much can you invest per year?
   - Does your employer offer a 401(k)?
   - If yes, do they match? By how much?
   - When do you plan to use this money? (5 years? 30 years?)
   - Are you looking for income or growth?
   - Are you interested in tax advantages?

2. Based on your answers, determine:
   - Should you open a 401(k) contribution? Why/why not?
   - Should you open an IRA? Traditional or Roth?
   - Should you also open a taxable brokerage account? Why?
   - What order should you open accounts in?

3. Write a paragraph explaining your account strategy

**Grading:** Complete if you've assessed your situation and planned your accounts

---

### Exercise 2.2: 401(k) Optimization (If Applicable)

**Objective:** Maximize your 401(k) benefits if your employer offers one

**Instructions:**
1. If your employer offers a 401(k):
   - Find your employer's 401(k) summary
   - What is the employer match percentage?
   - What is the vesting schedule?
   - What investment options are available?
   - What is your current contribution?

2. Calculate:
   - How much should you contribute to get full match?
   - What is the annual value of that match?
   - Example: If they match 50% up to 6%, and you earn $50,000:
     - Contribute $3,000 (6% of salary)
     - Employer contributes $1,500 (50% match)
     - That's a 50% instant return on your money!

3. If not currently maximizing match, create an action plan:
   - What do you need to change?
   - When will you make the change?
   - Set a reminder to do this

**Grading:** Complete if you understand your match and have a plan

---

### Exercise 2.3: IRA Decision

**Objective:** Decide if Traditional or Roth IRA is better for you

**Instructions:**
1. Research Traditional IRA:
   - Tax deduction now?
   - Taxes in retirement?
   - Contribution limit?
   - Can you withdraw early?

2. Research Roth IRA:
   - Tax deduction now?
   - Taxes in retirement?
   - Contribution limit?
   - Can you withdraw early?

3. Consider your situation:
   - What's your current income?
   - Do you expect higher or lower income in retirement?
   - How many years until retirement?

4. Make a decision:
   - Traditional IRA or Roth?
   - Why is this better for your situation?
   - Write 2-3 sentences explaining

5. Bonus: Calculate your potential wealth
   - Use a retirement calculator (search "IRA calculator")
   - Assume $6,500/year contribution
   - Assume 7% annual return
   - How much will you have at retirement?
   - This shows the power of tax-advantaged accounts!

**Grading:** Complete if you understand both types and made a choice

## Exercise Section 3: Reading Stock Quotes

### Exercise 3.1: Quote Scavenger Hunt

**Objective:** Become comfortable finding and reading stock quotes

**Instructions:**
1. Go to Yahoo Finance (finance.yahoo.com)
2. Search for each of these companies:
   - Apple (AAPL)
   - Microsoft (MSFT)
   - Amazon (AMZN)
   - Tesla (TSLA)
   - Coca-Cola (KO)

3. For each stock, write down:
   - Current price
   - Today's change ($ and %)
   - 52-week high/low
   - Market cap
   - P/E ratio
   - Dividend yield
   - Daily volume

4. Create a comparison table

5. Answer these questions:
   - Which stock is most expensive? (by price)
   - Which stock is most profitable? (by P/E ratio)
   - Which pays the most dividend?
   - Which stock is near its 52-week high?
   - Which stock is near its 52-week low?

**Grading:** Complete if you have data on all 5 stocks and answered questions

---

### Exercise 3.2: Depth of Quote Analysis

**Objective:** Understand what a quote tells you about a stock

**Instructions:**
1. Pick one stock (start with Apple - AAPL)
2. On your broker's platform, pull up the full quote
3. You should see many data points. Find and record:
   - Current price
   - Bid price and size
   - Ask price and size
   - P/E ratio
   - Earnings per share (EPS)
   - Revenue
   - Profit margin
   - Return on Equity (ROE)
   - Debt-to-Equity ratio

4. For each metric you found, research:
   - What does this number mean?
   - Is the current number good or bad?
   - How does it compare to industry average?

5. Write a one-paragraph analysis:
   - Based on the quote data alone, would you buy this stock?
   - What metrics made you think that way?
   - What other research would you want to do?

**Grading:** Complete if you've found and analyzed at least 8 quote metrics

---

### Exercise 3.3: Quote Interpretation Challenge

**Objective:** Interpret what quote data means in context

**Instructions:**

**Scenario 1: The Dividend Finder**
1. Search for these companies known for dividends:
   - Johnson & Johnson (JNJ)
   - Procter & Gamble (PG)
   - Coca-Cola (KO)
   - Verizon (VZ)

2. Find their dividend yields
3. Record which has highest yield
4. Research why that one has high yield:
   - Is the company stable?
   - Have they cut dividends recently?
   - Is the payout sustainable?
5. Would you buy it for the dividend? Why/why not?

**Scenario 2: The Growth Finder**
1. Search for these growth companies:
   - Nvidia (NVDA)
   - Tesla (TSLA)
   - Shopify (SHOP)
   - Adobe (ADBE)

2. Find their P/E ratios
3. Record which has highest P/E
4. Research the company:
   - Why is P/E so high?
   - Are earnings growing fast?
   - Is it overvalued or justified?
5. Would you buy it despite high P/E? Why/why not?

**Scenario 3: The Value Finder**
1. Search for these "value" companies:
   - JPMorgan Chase (JPM)
   - Ford Motor (F)
   - Bank of America (BAC)
   - Chevron (CVX)

2. Find their P/E ratios
3. Record which has lowest P/E
4. Research the company:
   - Why is P/E low?
   - Is it a good value or in trouble?
   - Is it cheap or fairly priced?
5. Would you buy it as a value play? Why/why not?

**Grading:** Complete if you've analyzed all 3 scenarios

## Exercise Section 4: Placing Your First Trade

### Exercise 4.1: Paper Trading

**Objective:** Practice trading without real money

**Instructions:**
1. Open your broker's paper trading account
   - Most brokers offer this for free
   - It simulates real trading with fake money
   - Perfect for practice

2. Make 5 practice trades:
   - Trade 1: Buy Apple (AAPL) - 10 shares
   - Trade 2: Buy Microsoft (MSFT) - 5 shares
   - Trade 3: Buy Coca-Cola (KO) - 8 shares
   - Trade 4: Sell 5 shares of Apple
   - Trade 5: Buy any stock you choose - 10 shares

3. For each trade, record:
   - Stock bought/sold
   - Number of shares
   - Price at entry
   - Order type used (market or limit)
   - Actual fill price
   - Why you chose this stock

4. Monitor your positions:
   - After 1 week, note the prices
   - Calculate your gains/losses
   - Don't obsess, just observe

5. Complete this reflection:
   - How did the process feel?
   - What was easiest?
   - What was hardest?
   - What surprised you?

**Grading:** Complete if you've made 5 trades and reflected on them

---

### Exercise 4.2: Pre-Trade Checklist Development

**Objective:** Create a process you'll use before every real trade

**Instructions:**
1. Based on Lesson 2.4, create your personal pre-trade checklist
2. It should include:
   - Account balance check
   - Stock research confirmation
   - Quantity decision
   - Order type selection
   - Price verification
   - Final confirmation

3. Print it out or save to your phone
4. Use this checklist for your first 5 real trades
5. After 5 trades, review:
   - Did the checklist help?
   - What would you change?
   - Update your checklist

**Grading:** Complete if you have a written checklist and have tested it

---

### Exercise 4.3: First Real Trade (When Ready)

**Objective:** Execute your first real trade

**Instructions:**
1. **Only do this when you're ready** - No pressure!
2. Research a company you believe in (not just random)
3. Fund your account with your chosen broker
4. Wait for deposit to clear (usually 1-3 days)
5. Execute your first trade:
   - Use your pre-trade checklist
   - Start small (maybe $100-500)
   - Use market order for simple execution
   - Take a screenshot of confirmation

6. Keep detailed records:
   - Date bought
   - Stock and quantity
   - Price paid
   - Total cost
   - Why you bought it

7. Monitor for one week:
   - Note price changes
   - Don't panic at normal volatility
   - Don't obsess either

8. Reflection:
   - How did it feel to execute a real trade?
   - Are you more confident now?
   - What would you do differently next time?

**Grading:** Complete if you've done paper trading and are ready for real trade
*(Real trades are optional - paper trading alone counts as complete)*

---

### Exercise 5.2: Paper Trading Different Order Types

**Objective:** Practice different order types without risk

**Instructions:**
1. Using your paper trading account:
2. Make these trades (using real prices at time of trade):

**Trade 1: Market Order**
- Buy 10 shares of Apple using market order
- Record: Limit price quoted vs. actual fill price
- Note: Difference (slippage)

**Trade 2: Limit Order**
- Try to buy 5 shares at a limit price BELOW current asking price
- Example: If ask is $195.48, try limit of $195.00
- Watch what happens over 1 hour
- Does it fill? Why/why not?

**Trade 3: Limit Order (Above Market)**
- Try to buy 5 shares at limit price ABOVE current asking price
- Example: If ask is $195.48, set limit at $196.00
- Does it fill immediately?

**Trade 4: Stop Order**
- Buy 10 shares of a stock using market order
- Set stop order 5% below entry price
- Watch it for 1 week
- Does it trigger? If so, when and why?
- If not, what happened to the stock?

**Analysis:**
- Write up what you learned:
  - Which order filled fastest?
  - Which gave you price control?
  - Which protected your position?
  - When would you use each?

**Grading:** Complete if you've executed all 4 trade types and analyzed them

## Exercise Section 6: Bid-Ask Spread and Liquidity

### Exercise 6.1: Spread Analysis

**Objective:** Understand how spreads vary across stocks

**Instructions:**
1. Look up bid-ask spreads for 5 different stocks:
   - 1 mega-cap (Apple, Microsoft, Google)
   - 1 large-cap (established company)
   - 1 mid-cap (growing but established)
   - 1 small-cap (smaller company)
   - 1 illiquid stock (penny stock or rarely traded)

2. For each, record:
   - Bid price
   - Ask price
   - Bid-ask spread ($ amount)
   - Stock price
   - Spread as % of price
   - Daily volume

3. Create a comparison table

4. Analysis:
   - Which has the tightest spread?
   - Which has the widest spread?
   - Is there a pattern? (Large companies vs. small?)
   - How much would spread cost on a $1,000 investment?

**Grading:** Complete if you have data on 5 stocks with spread analysis

---

### Exercise 6.2: Volume and Liquidity Check

**Objective:** Learn to check volume before trading

**Instructions:**
1. Develop a habit: Check volume on any stock before considering it
2. Practice by looking up these stocks:
   - Apple (AAPL) - highly liquid
   - Microsoft (MSFT) - highly liquid
   - Small company (choose one) - less liquid
   - Penny stock (under $5) - very illiquid

3. For each, find:
   - Daily volume
   - 3-month average volume
   - Is volume increasing or decreasing?
   - Recent price volatility

4. Decision making:
   - Which would be easiest to exit quickly?
   - Which would be hardest to exit?
   - Would you be comfortable holding each?
   - Why/why not?

5. Create a rule for yourself:
   - What minimum daily volume will you trade?
   - What situations would you make exceptions?

**Grading:** Complete if you've checked volume on 4 stocks and made a rule

---

### Exercise 6.3: True Cost of Trading Analysis

**Objective:** Calculate actual trading costs for different strategies

**Instructions:**
1. Calculate spread costs for these scenarios:

**Scenario 1: Conservative Long-Term Investor**
- Makes 5 trades per year
- Average stock price: $100
- Average spread: $0.05
- Shares per trade: 50
- Calculate:
  - Cost per trade?
  - Annual cost?
  - 30-year cost?

**Scenario 2: Active Trader**
- Makes 50 trades per year
- Average stock price: $80
- Average spread: $0.15
- Shares per trade: 100
- Calculate:
  - Cost per trade?
  - Annual cost?
  - 30-year cost?

**Scenario 3: Your Personal Trading Plan**
- How many trades per year do you plan?
- What will be your average stock price?
- Estimate your average spread (check 5 stocks)
- Estimate shares per trade
- Calculate:
  - Annual cost?
  - 30-year cost?
  - Does it matter? Why/why not?

**Reflection:**
- Are spread costs significant?
- How will you minimize them?
- Why is this important to understand?

**Grading:** Complete if you've calculated costs for all 3 scenarios

## Chapter 2 Final Project

### Comprehensive Trading Plan

**Objective:** Integrate all Chapter 2 learning into one plan

**Instructions:**
1. Create a comprehensive "Trading Plan" document including:

**Section 1: Account Selection**
- Which broker will you use? Why?
- Which accounts will you open? Why?
- When will you open each?

**Section 2: Trading Process**
- Describe your step-by-step trading process
- Include your pre-trade checklist
- Describe how you'll research stocks
- When will you use market vs. limit orders?

**Section 3: Risk Management**
- How much will you invest?
- What's your max loss per trade?
- How many positions will you hold?
- Will you use stop orders? When?

**Section 4: Liquidity Strategy**
- What minimum daily volume will you require?
- How will you check for this?
- Will you trade low-liquidity stocks? Why/why not?

**Section 5: Cost Awareness**
- What's your estimated annual spread cost?
- How will you minimize trading costs?
- Are there other costs to consider?

**Section 6: First 5 Trades**
- What stocks are you considering?
- Why each one?
- In what order would you buy them?
- How much in each?

**Section 7: Monitoring Plan**
- How often will you check your account?
- What will trigger a decision to sell?
- How will you avoid emotional decisions?
- Who will you talk to for advice?

2. Once written, review it:
   - Does it make sense?
   - Is it realistic?
   - Would you follow it?
   - Set a time to review after 3 months

**Grading:** Complete if you have all 7 sections written

## Tips for Success

**1. Don't Rush**
- These exercises are meant to be done over time
- Spend at least 2-3 hours per week
- You'll learn more if spread over weeks, not days

**2. Document Everything**
- Keep a trading journal
- Write down your thoughts and decisions
- This becomes valuable reference material

**3. Be Honest**
- Don't skip exercises because they're hard
- The hard ones teach you the most
- Building real trading skills takes effort

**4. Revisit Your Work**
- After 3 months, look back at your answers
- Have your views changed?
- What have you learned?

**5. Get Feedback**
- Share your trading plan with someone
- Ask for their thoughts
- Community feedback helps

**6. Paper Trade First**
- Always paper trade before real money
- This catches mistakes without cost
- Builds real confidence

**7. Start Small with Real Money**
- Your first real trades should be small
- Even $50-100 per trade is fine
- Size doesn't matter when learning

## Common Exercise Questions

**Q: Do I really need to do all of them?**
A: The more you do, the better you learn. At minimum, do exercises 1.1, 2.1, 3.1, 4.1, 5.1, and 6.1.

**Q: Can I skip paper trading?**
A: Strongly recommended against. Paper trading is risk-free practice. Why not do it?

**Q: What if I make mistakes in exercises?**
A: Great! Mistakes in practice prevent bigger mistakes with real money.

**Q: How do I know if I'm doing exercises right?**
A: There's no "perfect" answer for most exercises. As long as you're thoughtful and learning, you're doing it right.

**Q: Can I skip the final project?**
A: Not recommended. Your trading plan is the foundation for Chapter 3. You'll need it.
