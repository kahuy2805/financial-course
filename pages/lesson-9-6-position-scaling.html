Lesson 9.6: Position Scaling (Pyramiding and Averaging)

Learning Objectives

  • Understand position scaling techniques (pyramiding and averaging)
  • Apply scale-in strategies safely with position sizing
  • Avoid averaging down into losses
  • Manage scaled positions effectively

What is Position Scaling?

Position scaling is building into a position over time, adding more shares as price changes or targets are reached.

Key Types


Pyramiding (scaling up):







Averaging (scaling in):







Averaging down (dangerous):








Different goals:
Pyramiding (scaling up):
  • Add to WINNING positions
  • Each add is smaller than previous
  • Lock in some profit, keep upside exposure
  • Example: Buy 100 shares, add 50 on dip, add 25 on breakout
Averaging (scaling in):
  • Build position before price confirmation
  • Add at predefined levels during setup
  • Average cost down (but carefully!)
  • Example: Buy 50 shares, buy 50 more on dip, buy 50 more on bounce
Averaging down (dangerous):
  • Add to LOSING positions
  • Trying to lower average cost
  • Often increases losses
  • AVOID THIS - Causes ruin
  • Example: Buy 100 at $100, drops to $90, buy 100 more at $90
Different goals:
  • Pyramiding: Lock profit on winners, stay exposed
  • Averaging: Build better cost basis before move
  • Averaging down: Psychology (wrong thinking)

Pyramiding Strategy

Concept: Scaling Up Winning Positions


Position: Long MSFT at $350 (100 shares = $35,000)

Profit target: $380 (+8.6%)

Hold time: 5-10 days



Pyramid approach:

- Initial move: Price rallies to $360

- First pyramid: Add 50 shares (smaller than first 100)

- Move: Price rallies to $370

- Second pyramid: Add 25 shares (smaller than previous 50)

- Total position: 175 shares

- Average cost: Blended between $350, $360, $370



Calculation:

- Original: 100 √ó $350 = $35,000

- Pyramid 1: 50 √ó $360 = $18,000

- Pyramid 2: 25 √ó $370 = $9,250

- Total: 175 shares for $62,250

- Average cost: $62,250 / 175 = $355.71



Exit plan at $380:

- 175 shares √ó $380 = $66,500

- Profit: $66,500 - $62,250 = $4,250

- Return: 6.8% on capital deployed

- Would have been: 100 √ó ($380-$350) = $3,000 without pyramids

- Pyramid benefit: Additional $1,250 profit



Benefits:

- Profit from every stage of move

- Average cost lower than initial entry

- More shares working when price highest

- Each pyramid smaller = Reducing risk



Danger:

- If trend reverses at $370, extra shares lose money

- 175 shares √ó $10 stop hit = $1,750 loss vs. $1,000 on original

- Increased leverage increases drawdown

Pyramid Position Sizing Rules


Original position: 50 shares (100%)

Risk: 1.5% of account = $300



Pyramid 1: 25 shares (50% of original)

- Risk: 1.5% of account = $300

- Total risk so far: $600 (3% of account)



Pyramid 2: 12 shares (25% of original)

- Risk: 1.5% of account = $300

- Total risk so far: $900 (4.5% of account)



Pyramid 3: 6 shares (12% of original)

- Risk: $300

- Total risk so far: $1,200 (6% of account)



Pattern: Each pyramid is 50% of previous



Total position: 50 + 25 + 12 + 6 = 93 shares

Total capital invested: Depends on prices at each add

Total risk: $1,200 (6% of $20K account)



Control method:

- Each add is fixed risk ($300)

- Shares purchased = Risk / Stop distance

- Prevents over-leverage

- Maintains discipline



Stop loss management:

- Original stop: $340 for first 50

- Add pyramids only if stop still valid

- If one stop hit: Total loss limited

- Exit all if main trend reverses

Averaging Strategy (Building Positions)

Concept: Strategic Entry Averaging


Setup: High-conviction swing trade identified

Price action: Consolidation, waiting for breakout

Plan: Build position before move, not after



Entry plan:

Buy 1: At support ($100)

Buy 2: At mid-consolidation ($105)

Buy 3: On breakout ($110)



Execution:



Entry 1: Stock at support $99.50

- Buy 40 shares at $99.50 (cost: $3,980)

- Reason: Logical support, start building



Wait: Stock consolidates $100-$105, 3 days



Entry 2: Stock at $105 (mid-range)

- Buy 30 shares at $105 (cost: $3,150)

- Total: 70 shares at avg $101.86

- Reason: Confirming consolidation support



Wait: Stock continues consolidating



Entry 3: Stock breaks above $110 (breakout)

- Buy 20 shares at $110.50 (cost: $2,210)

- Total: 90 shares at avg $103.56

- Reason: Breakout confirmation, final add



Target: $125



Result at target:

- 90 shares √ó $125 = $11,250

- Cost basis: $9,340

- Profit: $1,910 (20.4% gain)

- Benefit: More shares working when confirmation comes



vs. Single entry:

- Buy 90 at $110 (breakout only)

- Cost: $9,900

- Profit at $125: $1,350

- Difference: Saved $560 by averaging earlier

Averaging Rules (Safety First)


DO:

‚úì Average into UPTRENDS (price rising)

‚úì Average at SUPPORT levels (technical confirmation)

‚úì Average with DECLINING position size (smaller adds)

‚úì Average with DECLINING risk per add ($300 max each)

‚úì Average when THESIS unchanged (conviction high)

‚úì Average with PROPER STOPS (protect each add)



DON'T:

‚úó Average into DOWNTRENDS (losing trades)

‚úó Average below moving averages (breaking trend)

‚úó Average with EQUAL position sizes (increases leverage)

‚úó Average without stops (reckless)

‚úó Average when THESIS BROKEN (revenge trading)

‚úó Average after hitting stop loss (averaging down!)



Example: Correct averaging in uptrend

- Uptrend: $90 ‚Üí $100 ‚Üí $110

- Support: $105 (pullback to support in uptrend)

- Add: Buy more at $105 support

- Risk: Limited (support holding, trend intact)



Example: Incorrect averaging in downtrend

- Downtrend: $110 ‚Üí $100 ‚Üí $90

- "Support": $95 (claimed support, but broken)

- Add: Buy more at $95 (averaging down!)

- Risk: UNLIMITED (trend broken, no support)

What NOT to Do: Averaging Down

Dangerous Strategy: Averaging Down Into Losses


The psychology:

- Buy AAPL at $100 (feels good)

- Price drops to $90 (feels bad)

- Think: "If I buy at $90, my average is $95" (feels better)

- Buy more shares at $90

- Price drops to $80 (feels terrible)

- Think: "If I buy at $80, average is $83.33" (feels better temporarily)

- Keep averaging down through $70, $60, $50



The math:

- 100 shares at $100 = $10,000

- 100 shares at $90 = $9,000 (total $19,000 = 200 shares)

- 100 shares at $80 = $8,000 (total $27,000 = 300 shares)

- Average cost: $27,000 / 300 = $90



The problem:

- Average cost looks better ($90 vs. $100)

- But position risk MULTIPLIED (300 shares vs. 100)

- Capital deployed TRIPLED ($27,000 vs. $10,000)

- If stock continues to $50: Loss = -$12,000 (63% of $19,000!)



Example of ruin:

- Original: 100 shares at $100, stop $80 = -$2,000 max loss

- Averaged down: 300 shares by $80, loss = -$9,000 (MUCH WORSE!)

- What was meant to be 2% loss became 47% loss!



The addiction:

- Lower average cost creates false hope

- Temporary relief from paper loss

- Each drop creates urge to average

- Eventually: Catastrophic loss that wipes account



The math trap:

- Psychologically: "Average cost is better"

- Financially: "Total loss is MUCH worse"

- Never average into losing position!

Why Averaging Down Fails


Reason 1: Thesis was wrong

- You were wrong on entry

- Stock keeps dropping

- Averaging down = Doubling down on wrong thesis

- Result: Catastrophic loss



Reason 2: Trend has reversed

- Stock was in uptrend at $100

- Now in downtrend, moving to $70

- Averaging assumes trend will reverse

- Trend rarely reverses at averaging points

- Result: False bottom, continue lower



Reason 3: Exponential loss acceleration

- Loss at $100: 0%

- Loss at $90: 10%

- Loss at $80: 20%

- Loss at $70: 30%

- Loss at $60: 40%

- Each level down = Larger % loss

- If you average at EACH level, position grows

- Total loss: -50% to -70% (RUIN!)



Reason 4: Capital exhaustion

- Can't average down forever

- Account capital limited

- Eventually no capital to average

- Forced to exit at WORST price

- Result: Largest loss possible



Rule: ACCEPT LOSSES

- Stop loss at $80 (2% loss)

- Exit there, move on

- Capital preserved for next trade

- Much better than averaging down to -50% loss

Stop Loss Management for Scaled Positions


3-entry position with different stops:



Entry 1 (50 shares @ $100):

- Stop loss: $95 (original $500 stop)



Entry 2 (30 shares @ $105):

- Stop loss: $98 (new entry support)



Entry 3 (15 shares @ $110):

- Stop loss: $105 (breakout support)



Position stop:

- How to set stops for 95 share total?

- Option 1: Multiple orders

  - 50 share stop at $95

  - 30 share stop at $98

  - 15 share stop at $105

  - Confusing, creates slippage



- Option 2: Single unified stop

  - Use average cost: $150.53 (wait, that's wrong number)

  - Use lowest support from any entry: $95

  - Stop all 95 shares at $95

  - Single order, clean execution



Better approach:

- If price breaks support, entire position fails thesis

- Exit all at once at lowest confirmed support

- Don't try to manage multiple exits on same position

- Simpler, cleaner, less emotional

Summary

Position scaling (pyramiding) adds to winning positions:

  • Pyramid up: Add smaller amounts as price rises
  • Average in: Build position at support levels
  • Each addition: Smaller than previous
  • Risk management: Fixed risk per add ($300)
  • Avoid: Averaging down into losses

Key principle: Scale into winners, not losers.

Key Takeaways

  1. Pyramiding: Add to WINNERS (prices rising)
  2. Averaging: Build at SUPPORT levels (planned)
  3. Each pyramid: Smaller than previous (declining risk)
  4. Fixed risk: $300 max per add (discipline)
  5. Never average down: Losing positions forbidden
  6. Track average cost: Important for position management
  7. Unified stops: Better than multiple stop orders
  8. Scale exits: Sell in stages, lock profits
  9. Maximum pyramids: Usually 3-4 (diminishing returns)
  10. Psychology: Feel the urge to average down? EXIT instead
