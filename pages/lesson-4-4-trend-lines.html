Overview

Trend lines are simple diagonal lines that connect highs and lows to show the direction and speed of an uptrend or downtrend. When you pair two trend lines together, they create a "channel" that contains price movement. In this lesson, you'll learn to draw trend lines, identify channels, and use them to predict future price movement.

Time to complete: 20-25 minutes

Prerequisites

  • Completed Lessons 4.1-4.3 (Price Charts, Timeframes, Support and Resistance)
  • Comfortable reading candlestick charts
  • Access to a charting platform (TradingView, your broker)

Core Content

What is a Trend Line?

A trend line is a diagonal line connecting two or more price points to show the direction of price movement.

Simple Definition:

  • Uptrend line: Connects rising lows (bottom of bounces)
  • Downtrend line: Connects falling highs (top of bounces)

Why Trend Lines Matter

Trend lines serve several purposes:

1. Show the Trend Direction

At a glance, you can see if market is going up, down, or sideways.

2. Predict Future Movement

If the trend line is valid, price should continue along it.

3. Identify Support/Resistance

The trend line itself acts as support (in uptrends) or resistance (in downtrends).

4. Signal Trend Changes

When price breaks the trend line, the trend may be ending.

Drawing Uptrend Lines

How to Draw an Uptrend Line

Step-by-Step:

  1. Find at least two rising lows

- Look for the lowest point of each bounce

- Select points where price bounced upward from

  1. Draw a line through these points

- Connect the first low to the second low

- Extend the line forward

- The line should slope UPWARD

  1. Validate with a third touch (optional but helpful)

- Ideally, price bounces off the trend line a third time

- Three touches = much stronger trend line

Step-by-Step:

- Connect the first high to the second high

- Extend the line forward

- The line should slope DOWNWARD

Trend Line Validity and Strength

Not all trend lines are equal. Some are stronger than others.

Factors That Make Trend Lines Stronger

1. More Touches


Weak: Price touched trend line 1 time

Strong: Price touched trend line 3+ times

2. Longer Duration


Weak: Trend line formed over 3 days

Strong: Trend line held for 3 months

3. Volume Confirmation


Weak: Bounces off trend line on low volume

Strong: Bounces off trend line on heavy volume

4. Perfect Alignment


Weak: Points touch line approximately

Strong: Points touch line precisely

Rating Trend Line Strength

Strong Trend Line:

  • 3+ touches to the line
  • Formed over weeks or months
  • High volume at bounces
  • Price respects the line consistently

Weak Trend Line:

  • Only 1-2 touches
  • Formed over days
  • Low volume
  • Price breaks it frequently

Channel Trading Strategy

When stock is in a channel, you have a clear trading system:

Upward Channel:


Buy at bottom line (support) â†‘ Expect bounce to top line

Sell at top line (resistance) â†“ Expect bounce to bottom line

Repeat while in channel

Downward Channel:


Short (sell) at top line (resistance) â†“ Expect bounce to bottom line

Cover (buy) at bottom line (support) â†‘ Expect bounce to top line

Repeat while in channel

Real-World Application

Scenario 1: Identifying an Uptrend Line

What You See on Daily Chart:


Apple (AAPL) over the last month:



Price moved as follows:

Day 5: Low at $150 (first bounce low)

Day 10: Low at $155 (higher low)

Day 15: Low at $160 (even higher low)

Your Action:

Draw an uptrend line connecting $150 â†’ $155 â†’ $160

Your Trading Plan:

  • Watch for future bounces from this trend line
  • When price approaches the line, prepare to buy
  • Place stop loss below the trend line
  • If price breaks below the trend line, the uptrend is ending

Scenario 2: Trading in an Upward Channel

Chart Setup:

  • Support line (lower): $100 (stock bounces up from here)
  • Resistance line (upper): $110 (stock bounces down from here)
  • Price is currently: $105

Your Trading Strategy:

When price approaches support at $100:


Buy 100 shares at $100

Stop loss: $98 (below support)

Target: $110 (resistance)

Potential profit: $10 Ã— 100 = $1,000

When price approaches resistance at $110:


Sell 100 shares at $110

Take your $1,000 profit

Or: Short at $110, expecting bounce to $100

Result: You make $1,000 every time you complete one cycle (buy at $100, sell at $110).

Scenario 3: Trend Line Break = Trend Change Signal

What Happens:


Stock had been following an uptrend line perfectly.

Price bounced off the line 3 times over 2 months.

On Day 60: Price falls BELOW the uptrend line!

What This Signals:

The uptrend is ending. The trend line no longer acts as support.

Your Trading Response:


Old position: SELL (the uptrend is over)

Short entry: Consider shorting (expect further decline)

Stop loss: Just above the broken trend line (old support)

Scenario 4: Channel Breakout

Chart Situation:

Stock has been in a horizontal channel:

  • Support: $150
  • Resistance: $160
  • Stock bounces between these levels for 3 months

What Happens:

Price suddenly breaks ABOVE resistance at $160 on high volume.

What This Signals:

The channel is breaking. Uptrend is starting.

Your Trading Response:


Action: BUY

Buy at: $160-162 (on the breakout)

Stop loss: $158 (just below resistance, which is now support)

Target: $170, $180, or higher (next resistance levels)

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Mistake #1: Drawing Trend Lines Incorrectly

What Traders Do Wrong:

"I'll draw a trend line connecting a high and a low, not two highs or two lows."

Why It's Wrong:

This doesn't follow the definition of uptrend (rising lows) or downtrend (falling highs).

The Right Way:

  • Uptrend line: CONNECT RISING LOWS
  • Downtrend line: CONNECT FALLING HIGHS

Mistake #2: Trusting a Trend Line After One Touch

Reality: One touch is not enough. A trend line needs at least 2-3 touches to be valid.

A trend line with only one touch is just a random line.

Mistake #3: Forcing Price to Fit a Trend Line

What Traders Do Wrong:

"I want there to be an uptrend, so I'll force a line that barely touches the prices."

Why It's Wrong:

The trend line should be obvious, not forced.

The Right Way:

Let price tell you where the trend line is. Don't force it.

Mistake #4: Not Adjusting Channels as Price Moves

Reality: As price moves, you may need to adjust your channel lines.

If price breaks out of a channel, the channel is invalid. Find a new one.

Mistake #5: Ignoring the Bigger Trend

What Traders Do Wrong:

"There's a small uptrend line, so I'll buy based on it."

Why It's Wrong:

What if the daily chart shows a downtrend? A small uptrend against the bigger downtrend often fails.

The Right Way:

Check the larger timeframe first. Trade with the main trend, not against it.

Quick Review

Trend Lines:

Uptrend Line:

  • Diagonal line connecting RISING LOWS
  • Slopes upward
  • Shows bullish direction
  • Acts as support (expect bounces up)

Downtrend Line:

  • Diagonal line connecting FALLING HIGHS
  • Slopes downward
  • Shows bearish direction
  • Acts as resistance (expect bounces down)

Trend Line Strength:

  • 3+ touches = strong
  • Longer duration = stronger
  • Higher volume = stronger

Channels:

  • Two parallel trend lines
  • Upper line = resistance/ceiling
  • Lower line = support/floor
  • Stock bounces between them

Channel Types:

  • Upward channel (bullish, both lines slope up)
  • Downward channel (bearish, both lines slope down)
  • Horizontal channel (sideways, both lines flat)

Using Trend Lines:

  • Trade bounces OFF the trend line
  • Exit when price breaks the trend line
  • Trend line break = trend change signal

Exercise 2: Drawing a Downtrend Line

  2. Identify three falling highs
  3. Draw a downtrend line connecting them
  4. Watch to see if price bounces off this line

Exercise 3: Identifying a Channel

  1. Find a stock trading in a range (not trending)
  2. Draw support line (connecting rising lows)
  3. Draw resistance line (connecting falling highs)
  4. Measure the distance between lines
  5. Develop a trading plan: Buy at support, sell at resistance

Exercise 4: Channel Trading Simulation

  1. How many times would you have bought at support? (count the bounces)
  2. How much would you have made per bounce?
  3. If you completed 5 bounces, what was total profit?

Exercise 5: Trend Line Break Detection

Find a stock that BREAKS an existing trend line (price goes through it).

  1. When did the trend line form?
  2. How many times did it hold?
  3. When did it break?
  4. What happened to the stock after the break?
  5. Was it a good signal to change your trading approach?
