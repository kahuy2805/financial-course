Lesson 7.5: Industry and Sector Analysis

Overview

Not all industries are created equal. Some industries have higher profit margins, more stable demand, and better growth prospects than others. Learn to identify which industries are hot and which are struggling, and you'll avoid the worst sectors and find the best opportunities.

Time to complete: 25-30 minutes

Prerequisites

  • Completion of Lesson 7.1-7.4
  • Understanding of competitive dynamics
  • Basic familiarity with different industries

Core Content

What is Sector Analysis?

Sector Analysis is the study of industry dynamics, competitive landscape, and trends that affect all companies in that industry.

Key Principle: Sometimes it's better to own a mediocre company in a great industry than a great company in a terrible industry.

The Market Sectors

The stock market is divided into 11 main sectors:


1. Technology (Apple, Microsoft, Google)

2. Finance (JPMorgan, Goldman Sachs, Bank of America)

3. Healthcare (Pfizer, Moderna, UnitedHealth)

4. Consumer Discretionary (Amazon, Nike, Disney, Starbucks)

5. Consumer Staples (Walmart, Costco, Procter & Gamble)

6. Industrial (Boeing, Caterpillar, Lockheed Martin)

7. Energy (ExxonMobil, Chevron, Shell)

8. Materials (Copper, Steel, Aluminum producers)

9. Real Estate (REITs, property companies)

10. Utilities (Electric, Gas, Water companies)

11. Communications (Verizon, AT&T, Meta, Netflix)

Sector Characteristics

Growth Sectors (Higher Growth, Higher Risk)


Technology:

- Characteristics: Fast growth, high margins, uncertain future

- Growth Rate: 10-20%+ annually

- P/E Ratio: Often 25-50 (expensive)

- Example: Nvidia grew 200% in 2023



Healthcare:

- Characteristics: Aging population, patent protection

- Growth Rate: 8-12% annually

- P/E Ratio: 15-30

- Example: GLP-1 drugs (Ozempic) driving growth

Mature/Stable Sectors (Lower Growth, Lower Risk)


Utilities:

- Characteristics: Essential service, regulated, stable

- Growth Rate: 3-5% annually

- P/E Ratio: 12-18

- Dividend Yield: 3-4%

- Example: Steady, predictable, boring (but safe)



Consumer Staples:

- Characteristics: People always buy groceries

- Growth Rate: 3-5% annually

- P/E Ratio: 15-25

- Dividend Yield: 2-3%

- Example: Walmart, Costco - recession-resistant

Cyclical Sectors (Boom/Bust Pattern)


Energy:

- When oil prices HIGH: Excellent profits

- When oil prices LOW: Poor profitability

- Highly dependent on commodity prices



Materials:

- When economy strong: Demand for copper, steel HIGH

- When economy weak: Demand collapses

- Closely tied to economic cycles



Industrial:

- When economy strong: Demand for equipment, construction HIGH

- When economy weak: Orders dry up

- First to be cut in recessions

How to Analyze a Sector

#### 1. Industry Growth Rate

What It Shows: Is the industry expanding or contracting?


High Growth Sector (10%+ annually):

- Technology

- Healthcare (certain segments)

- Renewable Energy

- Good for: Growth investors



Mature/Slow Growth (3-5% annually):

- Utilities

- Consumer Staples

- Good for: Income/value investors



Declining/Negative Growth:

- Print media

- Traditional retail

- Avoid: Risk of bankruptcy

#### 2. Competitive Landscape

What It Shows: How many competitors and how intense is competition?


Favorable (Few Strong Competitors):

- Apple in premium phones (strong brand moat)

- Microsoft in office software (switching cost moat)

- High margins possible



Unfavorable (Many Competitors):

- Airlines (commoditized, low margins)

- Retail (intense price competition)

- Gas stations (no differentiation, thin margins)

Real Example: Airline Industry Nightmare


Airlines:

- Many competitors (American, Southwest, Delta, United, etc.)

- Customers switch on price alone

- Fuel costs rise and cut into margins

- Result: Consistently low profit margins (2-5%)

- Better to avoid investing in airlines



Compare to:

- Apple (premium brand, few competitors)

- Microsoft (switching costs, enterprise lock-in)

- Result: High margins (35%+), great investments

#### 3. Industry Consolidation

What It Happening: Are competitors merging or is market becoming more fragmented?


Consolidating Industry (Good):

- Few large players remaining

- Smaller players go bankrupt or merge

- Survivors have pricing power

- Example: Telecom consolidation



Fragmenting Industry (Bad):

- New competitors enter easily

- Market share constantly in flux

- Price wars common

- Example: Food delivery apps (Uber Eats, DoorDash, etc.)

#### 4. Barriers to Entry

What It Means: How hard is it for new competitors to enter the market?


High Barriers (Good):

- Tech: Requires billions in R&D

- Pharma: Requires drug approval (10 years)

- Telecom: Requires infrastructure investment (billions)

- Result: Existing players protected, high margins



Low Barriers (Bad):

- Retail: Need some money but concept is simple

- Restaurants: Easy to start, many new entrants

- E-commerce: Can set up on Shopify in days

- Result: Easy competition, price wars, low margins

Sector Rotation Strategy

What Is It: Different sectors outperform in different economic conditions.

The Cycle:


Economic Recovery:

- Cyclical stocks first (Industrial, Energy, Materials)

- Money flows to growth stocks

- Tech and Healthcare lead



Expansion (Peak Growth):

- Tech and Consumer Discretionary peak

- High valuations, strong growth



Early Slowdown (Top):

- Defensive stocks rotate in (Utilities, Consumer Staples)

- Growth slowing, earnings misses

- Investors move to safety



Recession:

- Defensive stocks outperform (they hold value)

- Cyclicals collapse

- Safe havens (Treasury bonds, gold) rally

Real Example: 2008 Financial Crisis


Before Crisis (2007):

- Technology leading

- Financials hot

- Housing/Materials booming



During Crisis (2008-2009):

- Technology down 50%+

- Financials destroyed (banks collapsing)

- Consumer Staples and Utilities held value

- Who invested in Walmart when market crashed? Great returns!



Lesson: Cyclical stocks offer huge gains in recoveries

But Defensive stocks save you in crashes

Industry-Specific Metrics

Different industries use different metrics:


Technology:

- User Growth (Netflix subscriber adds)

- Market Penetration (% of addressable market)

- Gross Margins (CRITICAL - shows pricing power)



Banking:

- Return on Equity (ROE - how much profit on deposits)

- Net Interest Margin (NIM - profit on lending)

- Non-Performing Loans (bad loans on books)



Retail:

- Same-Store Sales Growth (growth from existing stores)

- Inventory Turnover (how fast inventory sells)

- Gross Margin by segment



Oil & Gas:

- Production Volumes (barrels produced)

- Oil Price Realization (actual price received)

- Reserve Replacement (new oil found)

Sector Valuation Benchmarks

Average P/E Ratios by Sector (These vary over time):


Highest Multiples:

- Technology: 25-35

- Healthcare: 20-30

- Consumer Discretionary: 18-28



Medium Multiples:

- Financials: 10-15

- Industrials: 12-18

- Materials: 10-15



Lower Multiples:

- Energy: 8-12

- Utilities: 12-18

- Staples: 15-25

Remember: Compare a company's P/E to its sector average, not market average.

Top-Down vs. Bottom-Up

Two Investment Approaches:

Top-Down (Sector First):


1. Identify best sectors (strong growth, high margins)

2. Identify best companies IN those sectors

3. Buy the best companies in the best sectors



Example: Healthcare growing, Pfizer is best in sector, buy Pfizer

Bottom-Up (Company First):


1. Find great companies (strong financials, moats)

2. Check what sector they're in

3. Buy great companies regardless of sector



Example: Love Apple's business, buy Apple (even if Tech sector weak)

Best Approach: Combination of both

  • Use sector analysis to avoid worst industries
  • Use company analysis to pick best in good sectors

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Mistake #1: Ignoring Sector Headwinds

Problem: You love a company's financials, but sector is in decline.

Solution: Even great companies struggle in dying industries. Avoid print media, traditional retail.

Mistake #2: Overweighting Hot Sectors

Problem: Tech is hot, so you buy 80% of portfolio in tech.

Solution: Diversify across sectors. Hot today = expensive today = trouble tomorrow.

Mistake #3: Betting Against Consolidation

Problem: Airline industry consolidating, flights fewer, prices higher. But industry still has low margins.

Solution: Don't assume consolidation = suddenly profitable. Some industries just have structural low margins.

Mistake #4: Not Adjusting Valuation for Sector

Problem: Tech stock has P/E of 50, consumer staples has P/E of 18. Buy staples thinking cheaper.

Solution: Tech deserves premium valuation. Compare to sector averages, not cross-sector.

Mistake #5: Missing Emerging Sectors

Problem: Ignore renewable energy thinking oil/gas forever. Miss 5-year, 10x move.

Solution: Watch for emerging trends. Today's speculative sector = tomorrow's mainstream.

Real-World Application

Sector Analysis Checklist

Before investing in a company, check its sector:

  • [ ] Is the sector growing or declining?
  • [ ] How many major competitors exist?
  • [ ] What are typical profit margins?
  • [ ] Is the sector consolidating?
  • [ ] What are the barriers to entry?
  • [ ] How does this compare to overall market?

Practice Exercise

Exercise 1: Sector Comparison

Pick two sectors (e.g., Technology vs. Utilities):

  1. What's the average P/E in each sector?
  2. What's the average ROE in each sector?
  3. What's the revenue growth rate for each?
  4. Which looks more attractive and why?

Exercise 2: Competitive Analysis

Pick a stock (e.g., Netflix):

  1. Who are the main competitors in the industry?
  2. How do their margins compare?
  3. Who has the best competitive advantage?
  4. Predict which will be biggest winner in 5 years

Exercise 3: Sector Rotation

Trace the stock market for the past year:

  1. Which sectors outperformed?
  2. Which underperformed?
  3. Can you identify the economic reason?
  4. Which sectors do you expect to lead next year?
