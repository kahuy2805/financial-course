Lesson 13.4: Tax-Efficient Trading Strategies

Overview

Taxes are your biggest drag on returns‚Äîoften larger than investment fees. A successful investor manages tax liability as carefully as asset allocation. The IRS wants its cut, but you can be smart about when and how you pay.

Tax Basics for Traders

Capital Gains Types

Short-Term Capital Gains (Assets held ‚â§ 1 year)


Tax Rate: Ordinary income rates (10%-37% federal, plus state)

Example: Sell stock after 6 months for $5,000 gain

         Your marginal rate: 24%

         Tax owed: $5,000 √ó 24% = $1,200



Effective Rate Examples (Federal Only):

$50,000 income:  22% tax = $1,100 on $5,000 gain

$100,000 income: 24% tax = $1,200 on $5,000 gain

$300,000 income: 35% tax = $1,750 on $5,000 gain

(Plus state taxes, additional 3.8% Net Investment Tax)

Long-Term Capital Gains (Assets held > 1 year)


Tax Rates: 0%, 15%, or 20% (much better!)

Example: Sell stock after 14 months for $5,000 gain

         Your bracket: 15%

         Tax owed: $5,000 √ó 15% = $750 (vs. $1,200 short-term)

         SAVINGS: $450 per $5,000 gain!



Rate Brackets (2024, federal):

0% rate:    Income up to ~$47,000 (single)

15% rate:   Income $47,000-$518,900

20% rate:   Income over $518,900



Effective Tax Savings:

Short-term 24% ‚Üí Long-term 15% = 9% saving per dollar

Short-term 35% ‚Üí Long-term 20% = 15% saving per dollar

Short-term 37% ‚Üí Long-term 20% = 17% saving per dollar

Dividend Tax Treatment

Qualified Dividends (from US stocks held 60+ days)


Taxed as long-term capital gains (0%, 15%, or 20%)

Most common for dividend stocks

Non-Qualified Dividends (from REITs, preferred stock, foreign stocks)


Taxed as ordinary income (10%-37%)

More expensive tax-wise

Tax Impact Examples:


$5,000 in Qualified Dividends @ 15% bracket:  $750 tax

$5,000 in Non-Qualified Dividends @ 24%:      $1,200 tax

DIFFERENCE: $450 (36% more expensive!)

Tax-Loss Harvesting: The #1 Tax Strategy

Basic Concept


Definition: Sell losing positions to realize losses,

which offset gains elsewhere



Example:

Gain from Apple stock: +$3,000 (taxable)

Loss from Tesla position: -$2,000 (deductible)

Net gain: $1,000 (only this is taxed)

Tax savings: $2,000 √ó 24% = $480!

Harvesting Workflow

Step 1: Identify Losses


Portfolio Review:

Position        Cost Basis   Current Value   Gain/Loss

Apple          $10,000      $11,500         +$1,500 (DON'T harvest)

Tesla          $5,000       $3,500          -$1,500 (HARVEST)

Google         $8,000       $7,200          -$800 (HARVEST)

Microsoft      $6,000       $9,000          +$3,000 (DON'T harvest)

Step 2: Calculate Offset Potential


Available losses: $1,500 + $800 = $2,300

Planned gains (from selling Apple): $1,500



Strategy:

1. Harvest Tesla loss ($1,500) ‚Üí Offset Apple gain

2. Save Tesla loss ($800) for next year



Result: Zero tax on the $1,500 gain!

Step 3: Execute Harvest Trades


Action Sequence:

1. Sell Tesla at $3,500 (realize $1,500 loss)

2. Wait 31+ days

3. Buy similar (but not identical) stock/ETF

   Alternative: Buy different diversified tech holding



4. Sell Apple at $11,500 (realize $1,500 gain)

5. Total tax: $0 (loss offset gain)



Cost: Small commission (typically $0-5) and small price drift

Wash Sale Rule (Critical!)

The Rule:


Can't deduct a loss if you buy substantially identical

security within 30 days BEFORE or 30 days AFTER the sale



If you violate: Loss is deferred (added to new cost basis)

Does NOT go away, just postponed

30-Day Window Visualization:


           30 days    SELL    30 days

           BEFORE    DATE    AFTER

           |---------|--------|---------|

    Can't buy    Loss harvest    Can't buy

    similar stock date here    similar stock



Total "safe zone": Sell, wait 31 days, buy same security

Avoiding Wash Sales:


Sold at loss:       DO NOT BUY:          CAN BUY:

Apple stock         Apple stock          Microsoft (diff company)

                    Apple options        Tech ETF (different holdings)

                                        Apple in DIFFERENT account

(VOO)              (SPY, IVV)           (VTI) - different index

                                        Russell 3000 (broader)



High-yield bonds   Same bond ETF        Different bond ETF

                   Same issuer bonds    Intermediate bonds

Asset Location Strategy: Where to Hold What

The Tax-Efficiency Hierarchy

Stocks and Stock Funds (low income, tax-efficient)

  • Taxed only on gains (when sold) and dividends (if qualified)
  • Best for: Tax-deferred accounts OR taxable if held long-term
  • Location: Roth IRA, 401(k), or Taxable accounts

Bonds and Bond Funds (high income, inefficient)

  • Taxed as ordinary income every year on interest
  • Worst in taxable accounts
  • Best Location: Traditional IRA, 401(k), tax-deferred accounts

REITs (high income, taxed as ordinary income)

  • Monthly/quarterly distributions taxed as ordinary income
  • Disastrous in taxable accounts
  • Best location: IRA, 401(k), or tax-deferred

Dividend Stocks (moderate income, qualified divs get preference)

  • Qualified dividends are tax-efficient
  • Can hold in taxable accounts (vs REITs/Bonds)
  • Best: Still better in tax-deferred accounts, but acceptable taxable

Growth Stocks (minimal income, capital appreciation)

  • No annual tax drag if held long-term
  • Most tax-efficient in taxable accounts
  • Can hold in taxable and defer taxes decades

Implementation Example


Total Portfolio: $500,000



Tax-Deferred Accounts (IRA, 401k): $250,000

- 50% Bonds: $125,000 (taxable interest is OK here)

- 30% REITs: $75,000 (high distributions OK here)

- 20% Dividend stocks: $50,000 (filler)



Taxable Account: $250,000

- 60% Growth stocks: $150,000 (buy and hold, minimal taxes)

- 25% Dividend stocks: $62,500 (qualified dividends preferred)

- 15% Municipal bonds: $37,500 (tax-free income!)



Result: Interest and REIT distributions in tax-deferred,

        Growth in taxable (minimal annual taxes)

Holding Period Management

Timing Sales for Tax Efficiency

Strategy 1: Hold Until Qualified (1+ year)


Bought: January 5, 2024

Sale Date Options:

- January 4, 2025: SHORT-TERM (one day too early!)

- January 5, 2025: LONG-TERM (exactly 1 year, tax-efficient)



Tax Difference:

If gain is $5,000:

- Short-term (24% rate): $1,200 tax

- Long-term (15% rate): $750 tax

- SAVINGS: $450 (just by waiting 1 day!)

Strategy 2: "Sell in December, Buy in January"


December (Current Year):

1. Sell winners to harvest long-term gains

2. Realize losses from underwater positions

3. Net gains reduced or eliminated



January (New Year):

1. Reinvest in similar holdings

2. Start new 1-year holding period

3. Portfolio remains similar, taxes minimized

Strategy 3: Stagger Sales Across Years


Problem: Realized $30,000 in gains in one year

Solution: Spread sales across two years



Year 1: Sell winners with $15,000 in gains

        Harvest losses to offset

        Net tax: Minimal



Year 2: Sell remaining winners with $15,000 in gains

        Lower overall tax impact

Tax-Advantaged Accounts Priority

Account Hierarchy for Deposits


PRIORITY 1: Take full employer 401(k) match

            (Free money! Don't leave it)



PRIORITY 2: Max out HSA if available

            (Triple tax advantage)

            - Pre-tax contribution

            - Tax-free growth

            - Tax-free medical withdrawals



PRIORITY 3: Max out Roth IRA

            ($7,000/year, $8,000 if 50+)

            (Tax-free growth forever)



PRIORITY 4: Max out 401(k) or solo 401(k)

            ($23,500/year, more if self-employed)

            (Deductible, tax-free growth)



PRIORITY 5: Contribute to taxable account

            (Use after tax-advantaged maxed)

Account Contribution Limits (2024)


401(k):              $23,500 ($31,000 if 50+)

IRA (Roth/Trad):    $7,000 ($8,000 if 50+)

HSA (Individual):   $4,150 ($5,150 if 55+)

Solo 401(k):        $69,000 (self-employed)

SEP IRA:            25% of net self-emp income

Mega Backdoor Roth: Up to $69,000/year

Tax Reporting and Documentation

Required Records


For each transaction:

- Purchase date

- Cost basis (price paid + commissions)

- Sale date

- Sale price

- Dividend dates and amounts

- Interest received

- 1099 forms from brokers

Using Cost Basis Methods

First-In, First-Out (FIFO)


Bought 100 shares at $50

Bought 100 shares at $60

Bought 100 shares at $70

Sell 100 shares



FIFO assumes: Sold the $50 shares

Result: Biggest gain! Higher taxes

Specific Identification


Bought 100 shares at $50

Bought 100 shares at $60

Bought 100 shares at $70

Sell 100 shares: "Specifically sell the $70 shares"



Result: Smallest gain! Lower taxes

Cost: Must track and declare specific lots



Most brokers now allow this (select at sale)

Average Cost Basis


300 shares worth ($50+$60+$70)/3 = $60 average



When selling 100 shares:

Cost basis: $60 √ó 100 = $6,000



Results: Middle ground between FIFO and specific ID

Tax Form 8949 and Schedule D


Form 8949: Reports each sale and capital gain/loss

Schedule D: Summary of gains/losses



Needed for:

- Stock/fund sales

- Option exercises

- Dividend income

- Interest income (if >$1,500)

Capital Loss Carryforward

Using Losses in Future Years


Situation:

Year 1: Realized $8,000 in losses, $3,000 in gains

        Net loss: $5,000

        Can deduct: $3,000 against income

        Carryforward loss: $2,000



Year 2: Realized $1,000 in gains

        Use $1,000 of carryforward loss

        Net taxable gain: $0

        Remaining carryforward: $1,000



Year 3: No gains, $5,000 regular income

        Use $3,000 carryforward loss (annual limit)

        Remaining carryforward: -$2,000

Key Rules:


- Can deduct up to $3,000 against ordinary income/year

- Excess carries forward indefinitely

- Use shortest holding period losses first (more valuable)

- Can offset unlimited capital gains

Key Takeaways

  1. Tax-loss harvesting can save 20-40% on realized gains
  2. Long-term capital gains are taxed 9-17% lower than short-term
  3. Asset location matters as much as diversification (bonds in IRAs!)
  4. Tax-deferred accounts compound faster (no annual tax drain)
  5. Track everything - cost basis, dates, amounts
  6. Qualified dividends are 9% cheaper than non-qualified
  7. One year holding period saves thousands over a lifetime
