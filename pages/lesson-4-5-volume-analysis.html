Overview

Volume is the number of shares traded during a specific period. It's the heartbeat of the market - showing whether a price move is backed by real conviction or just noise. In this lesson, you'll learn what volume means, how to read volume bars, and how volume confirms or questions price movements.

Time to complete: 20-25 minutes

Prerequisites

  • Completed Lessons 4.1-4.4 (Price Charts through Trend Lines)
  • Comfortable reading candlestick charts
  • Understand support/resistance and trend lines
  • Access to a charting platform showing volume

Core Content

What is Volume?

Volume is the total number of shares traded during a specific time period.

Simple Definition:

If you buy 100 shares of Apple and someone else sells 100 shares, that's a 100-share transaction. If 1 million such transactions happen in one day, the daily volume is millions of shares.

Why Volume Matters

Volume answers a critical question: Is this price move real, or is it just noise?

Scenario 1: High Volume Move (Real)


Price goes up from $100 to $110 on HUGE volume (5 million shares).



Interpretation: Many traders agreed to buy. This is a REAL move.

Likely continuation: Stock probably continues higher.

Scenario 2: Low Volume Move (Suspicious)


Price goes up from $100 to $110 on TINY volume (50k shares).



Interpretation: Only a few traders pushed it up. Not much conviction.

Risk: Stock might reverse when real trading begins.

Likely continuation: Move is questionable, could reverse.

The Key Principle:

High volume = Strong move (believe it)

Low volume = Weak move (be skeptical)

Volume Confirmation

Divergence: When Volume and Price Don't Agree

Divergence occurs when price moves up but volume goes down (or vice versa).

#### Rising Price, Falling Volume (Warning Sign)


Price rises from $100 to $120 over 3 days:



Day 1: Price $105   Volume 5 million  (bullish, high volume)

Day 2: Price $112   Volume 3 million  (price up but volume down!)

Day 3: Price $120   Volume 1 million  (price up but volume down more!)



Interpretation: DIVERGENCE - price rising on declining volume.

Warning: Fewer traders are buying as price rises.

Signal: Buyers are losing interest. Reversal likely soon.

Trading decision: Don't buy; wait for reversal down.

#### Falling Price, Drying Volume (Warning Sign)


Price falls from $120 to $80 over 3 days:



Day 1: Price $115   Volume 5 million  (selling, high volume)

Day 2: Price $100   Volume 2 million  (price down but volume down!)

Day 3: Price $80    Volume 300k       (price down but volume down more!)



Interpretation: DIVERGENCE - price falling on declining volume.

Warning: Sellers are losing interest. Selling is exhausting.

Signal: Downtrend might end soon, reversal up likely.

Trading decision: Prepare to buy on bounce.

Volume and Support/Resistance

Volume tells you the STRENGTH of support and resistance levels.

Strong Support (High Volume Support)


Stock falls to $100 and bounces up 3 times:



Each time it reached $100, volume surged (millions of shares).



Interpretation: Many traders remember $100 as strong support.

Strength: VERY STRONG - bounces will be reliable.

Trading plan: Buy at $100 with confidence, hold for bounce.

Weak Support (Low Volume Support)


Stock falls to $100 and bounces up 3 times:



But each time it reached $100, volume was low (hundreds of k).



Interpretation: Few traders remember $100 as support.

Strength: WEAK - bounce might fail if selling pressure continues.

Trading plan: Buy at $100 with CAUTION, smaller position.

Average Volume and Liquidity

What is Average Volume?

Average volume is the typical number of shares traded per day over a period (usually 20 or 50 days).


Apple (AAPL) average daily volume: 50 million shares

This Airline stock average daily volume: 2 million shares

Why Average Volume Matters

Liquidity (how easy it is to buy/sell) depends on average volume.

#### Liquid Stock (Easy to Trade)


Average volume: 50+ million shares per day



Advantages:

- Can buy/sell any size quickly

- Tight bid-ask spread

- Easy to enter/exit positions

- Price moves smoothly



Examples: AAPL, MSFT, GOOGL, TSLA

#### Illiquid Stock (Hard to Trade)


Average volume: Less than 1 million shares per day



Disadvantages:

- Hard to find buyers for your shares

- Wide bid-ask spread (5-10%!)

- Slippage (you buy at much higher price than expected)

- Price moves in jumps, not smoothly



Examples: Penny stocks, micro-cap stocks

Rule for Traders:

Only trade stocks with average volume above 1 million shares/day. Preferably 5+ million.

Volume and Price Breakdowns

When Price Falls Hard: Volume Tells the Story

Volume Spike Down (Selling Climax):


Price falls 10% on 10x normal volume.



Interpretation: Massive selling panic/climax.

What it means: All the sellers are selling at once.

Next likely move: BOUNCE UP (selling pressure exhausted).

Trading decision: Expect reversal up, buy weakness.

Price Falls on Low Volume (Lack of Interest):


Price falls 5% but volume is low.



Interpretation: Few sellers, mostly people absent.

What it means: No selling conviction, just drift down.

Next likely move: Could go either way.

Trading decision: Wait for volume to decide direction.

When Price Rises Hard: Volume Tells the Story

Volume Spike Up (Buying Frenzy):


Price rises 10% on 10x normal volume.



Interpretation: Massive buying frenzy/breakout.

What it means: All the buyers are buying at once.

Next likely move: BREAKOUT CONTINUES UP.

Trading decision: Buy on dips, expect more upside.

Price Rises on Low Volume (Lack of Interest):


Price rises 5% but volume is low.



Interpretation: Few buyers, mostly just offers being bought.

What it means: No buying conviction, just drifting up.

Next likely move: Likely reversal down when real selling appears.

Trading decision: Don't buy this weak rise.

Real-World Application

Scenario 1: Evaluating a Trading Setup

Chart Setup:

Stock price approaches resistance at $150 (where it bounced down 2x before).

Volume Analysis:

  • Previous bounces: Low volume (weak resistance)
  • Current approach: Volume is VERY HIGH and increasing

Your Assessment:

High volume + resistance = Strong reaction expected.

Your Trading Decision:

Place a short order above $150 (SELL if it breaks above).

Expect bounce down from resistance on this strong volume.

Scenario 2: Identifying a Weak Breakout

What Happens:

Stock breaks above resistance at $100 to $102.

Volume Check:

Volume is at 10-year LOW when it breaks through.

Your Assessment:

Breakout without volume confirmation = FAKE BREAKOUT.

Your Trading Decision:

DO NOT BUY. Watch to see if volume increases. If not, stock will reverse back below $100.

Scenario 3: Finding a Support Level to Trade

Chart Situation:

Stock has bounced from $80 level 5 times in past 3 months.

Volume Analysis:

Every bounce from $80 was on SURGING volume (millions of shares).

Your Assessment:

This is STRONG support with high-volume confirmation.

Your Trading Decision:

  • Buy at $80 with confidence
  • Stop loss: $78 (below support)
  • Target: $95 (next resistance)
  • Position size: Can be larger than usual (high confidence)

Scenario 4: Recognizing a Selling Climax

What You See:

Stock falls 15% in ONE DAY on 5x normal volume.

Volume Reading:

Massive red volume bar = Panic selling at climax.

Your Assessment:

All the sellers are probably done. Bounce is likely coming soon.

Your Trading Decision:

  • Don't panic sell (you'd be selling at worst prices)
  • Prepare to BUY on the bounce the next day
  • History says: Major down days on extreme volume often bounce hard

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Mistake #1: Ignoring Volume, Only Looking at Price

Reality: Price can move on very low volume, which is unreliable.

Always ask: "Is this move backed by volume?"

Mistake #2: Trading Breakouts Without Volume Confirmation

Reality: 70% of low-volume breakouts fail and reverse.

Rule: Only trade breakouts with volume increase.

Mistake #3: Assuming Volume = Good Trade

Reality: High volume can confirm either direction (bullish OR bearish).

Look at the DIRECTION of the volume, not just the amount.

Mistake #4: Trading Illiquid Stocks

Reality: With low average volume, you'll get terrible entry/exit prices.

Rule: Only trade stocks with 1M+ average daily volume.

Mistake #5: Forgetting Volume Can Mislead

Reality: Volume sometimes comes from forced selling (institutional fund closure, etc.), not fundamental changes.

Use volume as ONE piece of the puzzle, not the whole story.

Quick Review

Volume Fundamentals:

Volume: Total shares traded during a time period.

High Volume:

  • Price move is backed by conviction
  • Real buying or selling
  • Likely to continue

Low Volume:

  • Price move is not backed by real interest
  • Weak move
  • Likely to reverse

Volume Confirmation:

  • Breakout + high volume = Strong (buy it)
  • Breakout + low volume = Weak (skip it)

Divergence:

  • Price up but volume down = Warning sign, reversal likely
  • Price down but volume down = Selling exhaustion, bounce likely

Liquidity:

  • Average volume 1M+/day = Liquid (easy to trade)
  • Average volume <1M/day = Illiquid (hard to trade)

Key Principle:

Volume shows CONVICTION. No conviction = no trade.
