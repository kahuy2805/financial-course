Lesson 5.2: Fear and Greed - Your Biggest Enemies

Overview

While the emotional cycle has four stages, two emotions reign supreme in trading: Fear and Greed. These are the primary drivers of trading failure. Understanding their mechanics‚Äîand how to counteract them‚Äîis essential to long-term profitability.

Fear: The Emotional Paralyzer

What is Trading Fear?

Fear in trading is the primal instinct to protect yourself from loss. When you see money at risk, your brain's amygdala (the fear center) activates, triggering fight-or-flight responses that are completely inappropriate for trading.

The Two Types of Trading Fear

#### Type 1: Loss Aversion (Fear of Losses)

Definition: The pain of losing money feels twice as intense as the pleasure of making it.

This is a well-documented psychological phenomenon called "loss aversion" or "prospect theory," discovered by Nobel Prize winners Kahneman and Tversky.

The Numbers:

  • Losing $100 causes roughly 2x the emotional pain of gaining $100 in pleasure
  • This asymmetry makes traders close winners early and hold losers hoping for recovery

Real Trader Example: Sarah's Story

Sarah entered a stock at $100 with a plan:

  • Stop loss: $97 (3% risk = $300 loss)
  • Profit target: $106 (6% gain = $600 profit)
  • Risk/Reward: 1:2 (acceptable)

What happened:

  • Stock dropped to $98 (losing $200)
  • Sarah felt intense fear and anxiety
  • Instead of letting her stop work, she held, hoping for recovery
  • Stock dropped to $95, now she's down $500
  • To "make it back," she added more shares at $95 (averaging down)
  • Stock eventually hit $92, total loss: $800

What should have happened:

  • Stock hits $97, stop loss triggers automatically
  • Loss: $300
  • She moves to the next trade

The Result: Sarah's fear cost her an extra $500 and turned a planned, controlled loss into an emotional disaster.

#### Type 2: Regret Aversion (Fear of Missing Out / FOMO)

Definition: The fear that you'll miss an opportunity or make the "wrong" decision.

The Pattern:

  1. You pass on a trade setup
  2. The stock shoots up 10%
  3. You panic, thinking you "missed" the move
  4. You chase the stock at a much worse price
  5. It corrects, and you take a loss

Real Trader Example: Marcus Chases

Marcus had strict rules: Only enter on his defined setups. Stock XYZ was breaking out, but it didn't match his entry criteria (price action was too fast, volume was declining).

Marcus followed his rules and skipped it. Good decision.

Then XYZ went from $45 to $52 in 3 days. Marcus watched, increasingly frustrated. "I should have traded it," he thought.

By day 4, XYZ was at $51. Marcus threw aside his rules and bought at $51, thinking he still had a chance to catch the move. He told himself, "If I don't trade this, I'll regret it forever."

By day 6, XYZ pulled back to $46. Marcus was down $500 on an entry he never should have made.

The Lesson: FOMO trades are the most emotionally-driven and usually the worst performers.

How Fear Destroys Profitability

Fear creates a vicious cycle:

  1. You enter a trade with a good setup
  2. Price immediately goes against you (common‚Äîmarkets test your conviction)
  3. Fear activates: "What if I'm wrong?"
  4. You exit the trade early
  5. The trade goes to your target without you
  6. You feel regret, which increases FOMO
  7. Next time you see a similar setup, you over-trade to "make up for it"
  8. This over-trading results in emotional, revenge trades
  9. Losses mount, confidence drops
  10. You either quit or take excessive risks

Statistics on Fear-Based Trading

Research shows:

  • 73% of retail traders close profitable positions too early due to fear
  • 81% of traders who don't use stops (due to fear of "locking in losses") end up with account-destroying losses
  • Fear-driven traders underperform by an average of 3-5% per year vs. disciplined traders

Greed: The Confidence Killer

What is Trading Greed?

Greed is the belief that your winners will become bigger winners, or that losses will somehow magically recover. It's excessive confidence combined with the desire for more.

Types of Greed

#### Type 1: Target Expansion

Definition: Moving your profit target higher after the trade is already working.

The Problem:

You planned a 2% profit target. The stock hits 1.5%, and you think, "This is moving well, I could get 4%!" You adjust your target upward.

Now what happens if the stock pulls back 1.5%? You're suddenly sitting on a loss when you should have had a guaranteed win.

Real Trader Example: Jennifer's Greed

Jennifer entered at $50 with a target of $52 (200 profit, 4% gain).

Stock went to $51.50. Jennifer thought: "This is moving nicely. Why not $53? That's 6%, which would be $300 profit instead of $200."

She moved her target to $53 and adjusted her stop to breakeven ($50.01).

Stock rallied to $52.50, but then pulled back to $50.20. Jennifer got stopped out with a tiny loss after watching a $150 profit slip away.

The Math:

  • Original plan: Win $200, Risk $300 (not ideal, but disciplined)
  • Actual result: Lose $20, and miss the original profit
  • Cost of greed: She gave back $220 in potential gain

#### Type 2: Revenge Sizing

Definition: Increasing position size after losses to "make back" the money quickly.

The Problem:

After a loss, your brain wants to recover it immediately. So you take a bigger position on the next trade. This violates the first rule of trading: Fixed position sizing based on risk, not emotions.

Real Trader Example: Derek's Revenge Trade

Derek's account: $10,000

Trade 1: Lost $500 (normal, within his plan)

Derek feels the loss emotionally and wants to make it back quickly.

Trade 2: Instead of risking his normal $500, Derek risks $1,500 to "make it back faster"

Result: Lost another $1,000

Now Derek is down $1,500 and panicking. He takes another oversized trade to recover.

Result: Lost $2,000

Derek's $10,000 account is now $6,500. In three revenge trades, he lost 35% trying to make back 5%.

The Pattern of Revenge Sizing:


Initial Loss: -$500

#### Type 3: Holding Through Profit Targets

Definition: Refusing to take profits because you think the move will continue further.

The Problem:

You reach your profit target, but you're now in a greedy mindset: "This was only a 2% move. Let it run to 5%!"

Most of the time, you're right that it could run further. But sometimes‚Äîoften‚Äîit doesn't. And when it reverses, you lose the profit you already had in hand.

Real Trader Example: Tom's Greed at the Target

Tom's trade hit his $1,000 profit target at $55. He had planned to exit.

But Tom looked at the chart and thought the momentum could continue. He held instead of selling.

Stock went to $56 (+$200 additional profit, total +$1,200).

Then it dropped to $54 (-$200 from peak, but still +$800 profit from entry).

Tom, now anxious, sold at $54 for $800 profit.

Tom made $800 instead of $1,000. The "greed" cost him $200, plus the mental stress of watching his profit shrink.

The Real Cost:

  • Expected outcome: $1,000
  • Actual outcome: $800
  • Emotional cost: High (watching gains evaporate)
  • Psychological impact: "If I'd just sold at target, I'd have made more and felt better"

Fear vs. Greed: The Losing Combination

The worst traders cycle between fear and greed multiple times per day:

  1. 9:45 AM - Greed: Stock is up, trader holds instead of taking profit
  2. 10:15 AM - Fear: Stock pulls back 0.5%, trader panics and sells
  3. 10:30 AM - Regret: Stock rallies again, trader has FOMO
  4. 11:00 AM - Greed: Trader re-enters on FOMO, oversized
  5. 11:45 AM - Fear: Stock turns against him, trader takes a loss
  6. 12:30 PM - Revenge Greed: Trader takes oversized position to "make it back"
  7. 1:15 PM - Massive Fear: Trade goes against him significantly, trader takes a large loss

By 1:15 PM, this trader has turned what should have been a $1,000 day into a $3,000 loss through emotional cycling.

The Science: Why Fear and Greed Are Hard-Wired

Your brain didn't evolve for trading. It evolved for survival.

The Caveman Brain

500,000 years ago, losing $300 meant you died. Greed for more food meant you survived the winter. These were survival mechanisms.

But in modern trading:

  • A small loss is information, not a threat to survival
  • Greed for "one more 2%" often results in giving back all profits

Your ancestors needed fear to survive predators.

You need discipline to survive markets.

Neurochemistry

When you have a winning trade:

  • Your brain releases dopamine (the reward chemical)
  • This feels amazing
  • It triggers the desire for more
  • This is the neurochemical basis of greed

When you have a losing trade:

  • Your brain releases cortisol and adrenaline (stress chemicals)
  • This feels terrible
  • It triggers the desire to escape
  • This is the neurochemical basis of fear

The problem: These chemicals are designed to make you feel good/bad, not to make you profitable.

Counteracting Fear and Greed

Strategy 1: Pre-Commitment

Write your rules in stone BEFORE you trade.

Create a "Trading Constitution" that you sign:


I commit to:

- Taking ALL profits at my pre-defined targets

- Taking ALL losses at my pre-defined stops

- Not adjusting targets/stops once a trade is entered

- Exiting oversized trades immediately

- Never revenge trading

- Risking only 1-2% of my account per trade

Print this and put it next to your monitor. Before every trade, read it. Before every exit decision, re-read it.

Strategy 2: Automation

Use broker alerts and limit orders to execute trades mechanically.

Example:

  1. You identify a buy setup at $50 with target at $52
  2. You enter a BUY order at $50
  3. You set a SELL limit order at $52 (profit target)
  4. You set a SELL stop order at $48 (stop loss)
  5. You step away from the monitor

Now your emotions can't interfere because your exit is already predetermined and automated.

Strategy 3: Risk Management

The single most important rule: Risk only 1-2% of your account per trade.

If you risk 1% per trade:

  • Even 10 losses in a row costs you only 10% of your account
  • You're still alive to trade tomorrow
  • Greed-based oversizing becomes impossible because you literally can't fit a large position

Example:

  • Account: $10,000
  • Max risk per trade: $100 (1%)
  • Stock entry at $50, stop at $47 (3% loss)
  • Max position: 333 shares (333 √ó $3 loss = $999 exposure)
  • Even if all 10 trades are losers: $10,000 - $1,000 = $9,000 (90% intact)

Strategy 4: Trading Journal

Document what you felt during each trade. Track:

  • What emotion did you experience at entry?
  • What emotion at drawdown?
  • Did you follow your plan or deviate?
  • What was the financial cost of each deviation?

Over time, you see the pattern: "Every time I feel greed, I lose money."

This awareness, built through journaling, creates behavioral change.

Strategy 5: The Waiting Game

If you're unsure whether to hold or exit, wait 5 minutes.

Most emotional decisions feel different after 5 minutes. If you still want to exit or hold after 5 minutes of waiting, that's when you make the decision.

The 5-minute rule prevents emotion-driven decisions that you'll regret.

Real-World Case Study: The $50,000 Lesson

Trader: Michael (10 years experience, should know better)

Account: $50,000

Week 1:

  • Trade 1: Won $800 (greed‚Äîheld too long but still won)
  • Trade 2: Won $1,200 (discipline‚Äîtook target as planned)
  • Trade 3: Lost $600 (normal variance)

Week 1 Result: +$1,400

Week 2:

  • Trade 4: Down 0.5%, Michael feared losing his week's gains. Exited early. Lost $200 potential profit.
  • Trade 5: Wanted to make back the $200. Took oversized position (greed). Lost $1,500.
  • Trade 6: Revenge trade (greed + fear). Lost $2,200.
  • Trade 7: Revenge trade. Lost $1,800.

Week 2 Result: -$5,700

What Happened:

Michael let fear of losing his profits + greed for recovery drive a cascade of bad decisions. One week of discipline was followed by one week of emotion, resulting in a 4:1 loss ratio (lost 4x more than he made).

The Cost:

  • If Michael had stuck to his plan: ~+$1,200 for the week
  • What he actually got: -$4,300 for the week
  • Total cost of emotional trading: $5,500 swing

Key Takeaways

  1. Fear and Greed are not character flaws‚Äîthey're biological responses designed for survival, not trading
  2. Fear causes premature exits and revenge sizing; Greed causes oversizing and target expansion
  3. The solution isn't to eliminate fear/greed‚Äîit's to remove decisions from emotional moments
  4. Use pre-commitment, automation, and risk management to prevent emotional override
  5. Risk 1-2% max per trade to make greed-based oversizing mathematically impossible
  6. Your trading journal will reveal the exact cost of emotional deviations
