Core Content

The Three Financial Statements

Every publicly traded company publishes three statements quarterly and annually:

  1. Income Statement - Profit and Loss
  2. Balance Sheet - Assets and Liabilities
  3. Cash Flow Statement - Cash Movement

Statement 1: Income Statement (P&L)

What It Shows: Revenue, expenses, and profit over a period (quarter or year).

The Formula:


Revenue (top line)

- Cost of Goods Sold

= Gross Profit

- Operating Expenses

= Operating Income

- Interest Expense

= Income Before Taxes

- Taxes

= Net Income (bottom line)

Statement 3: Cash Flow Statement

What It Shows: Where cash came from and where it went during the period.

Why It's Important: Profit on the income statement is often not the same as cash in the bank.

Real Example: Profit vs. Cash

Income Statement:

- Net Income: $500 (profit)

- Operating Cash Flow: $0 (customer hasn't paid yet!)



Difference: Company shows $500 profit but has $0 cash

This is why cash flow matters!

Key Metrics on Cash Flow:

| Metric | What It Means | What You Want |

|---|---|---|

| Operating Cash Flow | Cash generated from business | Growing and positive |

Reading the Cash Flow Statement:

Question 1: Is the company generating cash?


Operating Cash Flow: $100M

- This is the most important number

- If positive and growing, company is healthy

- If negative, company is burning cash

Question 2: How much free cash flow?


Operating Cash Flow: $100M

Capital Expenditures: $20M (needed for growth)

Free Cash Flow: $80M



Free Cash Flow / Revenue = 80/500 = 16%

Good benchmark: 5-15% for healthy companies

This company is at upper end (EXCELLENT)

Question 3: What's the company doing with excess cash?


Option A: Paying dividends (returning to shareholders)

Option B: Reinvesting (building factories, R&D)

Option C: Paying down debt (strengthening balance sheet)

Option D: Buybacks (rewarding shareholders)



Best answer: Mix of all four depending on company stage

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Mistake #1: Only Looking at Net Income

Problem: Company has $10M profit, so you think it's good. You don't check if cash flow is negative.

Solution: Check all three statements. Profit means nothing if cash flow is negative.

Mistake #2: Ignoring Changes in Working Capital

Problem: Revenue growing fast, but accounts receivable ballooning. Cash isn't coming in fast enough.

Solution: Compare revenue growth to cash flow growth. Should be roughly aligned.

Mistake #3: Confusing GAAP and Non-GAAP

Problem: Company reports two different profit numbers. Not sure which is real.

Solution: Use GAAP (official) numbers. Non-GAAP often excludes bad items to look better.

Mistake #4: Not Adjusting for One-Time Items

Problem: Company had one-time gain on sale of asset. Profits look inflated.

Solution: Remove one-time gains/losses to understand recurring profitability.

Mistake #5: Ignoring Depreciation and Amortization

Problem: Company has big depreciation, so accounting profit is low. But it's non-cash.

Solution: Understand depreciation is non-cash. Add it back to understand true cash generation.

Real-World Application

Where to Find Financial Statements

SEC Edgar Database (Free, official)

  • sec.gov/cgi-bin/browse-edgar
  • All 10-Q (quarterly) and 10-K (annual) filings
  • Most accurate source

Company Investor Relations

  • investor.[company].com
  • Usually more readable format
  • Same data as SEC

Financial Websites

  • Yahoo Finance, Google Finance, Seeking Alpha
  • Simplified format
  • Good for quick reference
