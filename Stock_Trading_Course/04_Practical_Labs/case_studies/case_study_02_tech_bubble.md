# Case Study 02: Tech Bubble Burst (2021-2022) - Valuation Collapse

## Executive Summary

From November 2021 to December 2022, growth-heavy technology stocks crashed 67% while the broader S&P 500 fell 19%. This case study examines how valuations become disconnected from reality, why concentrated bets in "obvious winners" fail, and how to identify bubble markets before they burst.

**Time Period**: November 2021 - December 2022
**Nasdaq Decline**: -67% from peak (November 2021 to October 2022)
**S&P 500 Decline**: -19%
**Key Lesson**: Valuations matter, concentrating in bubbles destroys wealth faster than markets recover it

## The Bubble Analysis: How Valuations Collapsed

### Valuation Compression

**Price-to-Earnings Multiple Contraction:**

```
                    Nov 2021    June 2022    Dec 2022
Tesla PE            76.8x       11.2x        33.2x (still high)
Nvidia PE           58.4x       42.3x        43.1x (still expensive)
ARKK PE             79.5x       24.5x        19.3x
S&P 500 PE          21.5x       17.8x        16.2x
```

### Why Valuations Collapsed

**Root Cause 1: Fed Policy Reversal**
- 2021: Fed funds rate = 0%, QE ongoing
- 2022: Fed raises rates 4.25% (4 points), halts QE, discusses QT
- **Impact**: Discount rate for future earnings increases
- **Math**: Future cash flows worth less with higher rates

**Example with Tesla:**
- At 0% risk-free rate: Future earnings worth much more (90+ PE justified)
- At 2.5% risk-free rate: Future earnings worth much less (15-20 PE fair value)
- Tesla's earnings didn't change, but the denominator in valuation equation changed dramatically

**Root Cause 2: Slowing Growth Prospects**
- Zoom: From $35B revenue growth to maturation
- Roku: Slowing ad market
- Crypto (Coinbase): Crypto winter reduced trading volume

**Root Cause 3: Inflation and Recession Fears**
- Inflation hit 40-year high in June 2022
- Rates rising faster than expected
- Recession risk increased
- Growth stocks suffer most in recession fears

**Root Cause 4: Margin Call Cascades**
- Leveraged investors like Patricia forced to sell
- Sells at any price to meet margin requirements
- Creates "death spiral" of declining prices and more forced selling

### The Valuation Framework: Why Bubbles Form

**Bubble Formation Cycle:**

```
Stage 1: Disruptive innovation emerges
  - Real business, real growth potential
  - Early investors make excellent returns
  - ARKK +88% in 2021 was based on real outperformance

Stage 2: FOMO and money floods in
  - Crowd follows successful investors
  - Price rises faster than fundamentals
  - Valuations reach 2-3x normal levels
  - Patricia adds margin to buy ARKK at $65 (already up 88%)

Stage 3: Valuations become absurd
  - Price-to-sales, price-to-revenue reach historic extremes
  - Narrative overrides math
  - "Valuations don't matter" becomes common phrase
  - November 2021: Zoom trading at 118x earnings

Stage 4: Catalyst breaks the spell
  - Interest rate hike (December 2021)
  - Earnings miss
  - Profit-taking
  - Something makes investors question assumptions

Stage 5: Crash and capitulation
  - Leveraged investors forced to sell
  - Margin calls cascade
  - Most violent selling when sentiment flips
  - June 2022: ARKK, Tesla, Netflix all limit down

Stage 6: Recovery (that takes years)
  - Valuations compress to reasonable levels
  - Growth eventually justifies the valuation
  - Or companies fail and valuations drop further
  - 2023-2024: Tech recovery begins, but not back to peaks
```

## Lessons Learned

### Lesson 1: Valuation Metrics Matter

**Patricia's Mistake:**
- Ignored that ARKK was trading at 79x earnings (vs 20x normal)
- Ignored that Zoom at 118x earnings was unsustainable
- Ignored that Tesla at 76x earnings left no room for disappointment
- Thought "quality companies" made valuations irrelevant

**The Reality:**
- No company grows fast enough to justify 80x earnings forever
- At some point, high valuations require exponential growth
- Exponential growth is impossible (mathematical reality)
- Valuations eventually revert to means

**Rule for Valuation Discipline:**
```
Fair Value PE (S&P 500):        16-18x earnings
Growth Premium (Tech):          20-25x for fast growers
Bubble Level:                   40x+ (extreme caution)

If PE > 50x:
- Position size: Maximum 5% of portfolio
- Conviction: Very high, but sizing reflects risk
- Leverage: Never with borrowed money
```

**Application:**
Patricia's position was:
- 33% of portfolio in ARKK (79x PE)
- 27% of portfolio in Tesla (76x PE)
- Plus leverage on top

This violated every principle of risk management.

### Lesson 2: Leverage Amplifies Both Gains AND Losses

**The Leverage Math:**

Patricia's position at peak (with margin):
- Principal: $450,000
- Margin: $50,000
- Leverage ratio: 1.11x

If portfolio falls 50%:
- Without leverage: $450,000 → $225,000 (lose $225,000)
- With leverage: $500,000 invested → $250,000 value, minus $50,000 margin debt = $200,000 net (-$250,000 loss)

Patricia's actual loss was worse because:
- She kept adding margin (increased leverage to 1.15x)
- Margin calls forced selling at worst prices
- Margin debt remained even after portfolio collapsed

**Leverage Rule:**
```
Leverage Ratio of 1.0 (no margin):
  - 50% crash: Lose $225,000 (50% of $450,000)
  - Can weather it

Leverage Ratio of 1.11x (moderate margin):
  - 50% crash: Lose $250,000 + margin calls
  - Forced to sell at worst prices
  - Margin debt remains

Leverage Ratio of 1.5x+ (aggressive):
  - 50% crash: Forced immediate liquidation
  - Total wipeout
```

**Patricia's Fatal Error:**
> "ARKK was down 70%, a discount. I bought more on margin at $65/share thinking I was smart. I was actually doubling down on a losing position with borrowed money."

This violated every risk management principle.

### Lesson 3: Concentration Risk Kills Portfolios

**Concentration Analysis:**

Patricia's portfolio (Nov 2021):
- 33% ARKK (single ETF)
- 27% Tesla (single stock)
- 18% Nvidia (single stock)
- 11% Coinbase, Roku, Zoom (single stocks)
- Only 11% in broad diversification (SPY)

**Concentration in a bubble means:**
- All positions correlated (all crashed together)
- No hedge against growth collapse
- No safety net when thesis breaks

Marcus's portfolio (Nov 2021):
- 56% Broad US market (SPY)
- 22% Tech-heavy allocation (QQQ)
- 17% Healthcare/Utilities (defensive)
- 6% International (diversification)

**Result:**
- Marcus's Nasdaq positions fell faster than S&P 500
- But SPY holdings and defensive positions protected him
- Net portfolio down 14% vs Patricia down 78%

**Concentration Rule:**
```
Position Size Guidelines:
- Cash/Bonds: 0-40% (based on risk tolerance)
- Single stocks: 5-10% max each
- Sector concentration: 30% maximum
- Alternative assets: 10% maximum
- Any single "theme": 25% maximum

If investing in bubbles:
- High-valuation positions: 5% max
- Multiple high-valuation positions: Even smaller
- Never leverage a bubble position
```

Patricia violated all of these.

### Lesson 4: Adding to Losing Positions in Bubbles Is Dangerous

**The Averaging Down Fallacy:**

Patricia's thinking (January 2022):
> "ARKK is down from $121 to $75. That's a 38% discount. The same companies, cheaper prices. I should add more. This is what you're supposed to do - buy low."

**The Problem:**
In a bubble burst, you're not buying low - you're buying a broken thesis:
- ARKK was down because valuations were contracting
- The companies weren't cheaper, they were overpriced
- Adding to a bubble bursting position = trying to catch a falling knife

**The Difference:**

**Averaging down WORKS when:**
- Fundamentals are strong
- The decline is temporary/sentiment-driven
- Valuation is reasonable
- You have spare capital for additional risk

**Averaging down FAILS when:**
- The decline is from a bubble peak
- Valuations are still high
- You're using leverage
- You're adding to the worst performers

Patricia averaged down on:
- ARKK (worst performer, -74% by year-end)
- Tesla (worst performer, -90% by year-end)
- Both using leverage

**Rule:**
```
If a position is in bubble territory (80x PE):
- Never average down into a decline
- Never use leverage to add
- If you own it, take profits on rallies
- If you want exposure, buy gradually at normal valuations
```

### Lesson 5: Margin Calls Destroy Discipline

**How Margin Calls Work:**

Patricia's broker required 30% margin maintenance (industry standard is 25-30%).

When portfolio drops, broker calculates:
```
Margin requirement = (Margin debt) / (Total portfolio value)

If portfolio is $500,000 (includes $50,000 margin):
- Maintenance needed: $50,000 / $500,000 = 10% (OK)

If portfolio drops to $200,000:
- Requirement: $50,000 / $200,000 = 25% (Getting close)

If portfolio drops further to $150,000:
- Requirement: $50,000 / $150,000 = 33% (MARGIN CALL!)
- Must pay $5,000 or sell $20,000 of securities
```

**The Death Spiral:**
- Margin call forces sale
- Sale at worst market prices
- Sale creates more selling pressure
- Prices drop further
- Another margin call
- Repeat until account is liquidated

This is exactly what happened to Patricia:
- March margin call → forced to sell best performers (ARKK)
- Selling ARKK pushed price down
- Portfolio falls more
- April margin call → forced to sell Tesla
- Selling Tesla pushed price down
- Portfolio falls more
- June forced liquidation

**Margin Rule:**
```
Safe margin usage: Maximum 10% of portfolio
- Borrow less than 10% of portfolio value
- Maintains 90% safety buffer
- Very unlikely to trigger margin calls

Moderate margin: 10-20% of portfolio
- Higher risk
- Requires monitoring
- Can handle 40% market decline

Aggressive margin: 20%+ of portfolio
- Dangerous in any downturn
- Likely to trigger calls
- Should only be used by professionals

Patricia's situation: 11% margin + adding to it = 15%+
- Used on bubble stocks (highest risk)
- Added to losing positions
- Resulted in forced liquidation
```

## Key Takeaways

### 1. Valuations Do Matter
High valuations (80x PE) aren't justified by any growth rate. Eventually, reality catches up.

### 2. Concentration + Leverage = Portfolio Destruction
Patricia's 60% allocation to ARKK + Tesla with leverage turned a bad year into a catastrophic one.

### 3. Bubbles Are Easier to Spot in Hindsight
But you can identify bubble conditions:
- Valuations at multi-decade extremes
- Narratives replacing analysis ("disruption justifies any price")
- Leverage creeping in as accessibility
- Retail money flooding in
- FOMO in conversation

### 4. Margin on Bubble Stocks Is Unforgivable Risk
Margin works fine for stable positions, but on 80x PE stocks, it's gambling with forced-loss triggers.

### 5. Diversification Prevents Catastrophe
Marcus's diversification cost him 4% of returns during bubble (2021), but saved him 64% of losses during crash (2022).

### 6. Recovery Takes Time
Even great companies need years to recover from bubble valuations. Patricia could have been a 5-10 year holder, but forced liquidation removed that possibility.

### 7. Following Momentum Into Bubbles Kills Returns
Adding to ARKK at $65 (after 88% gain) was following momentum, not rational investing.

## Valuation Checklist for Your Portfolio

Before holding a position at bubble valuations, ask:

- [ ] Can I explain the PE ratio to a 12-year-old?
- [ ] Would I buy this at 2x the current price?
- [ ] Is the position 5% or less of my portfolio?
- [ ] Am I using zero leverage on this position?
- [ ] Have I considered what happens if growth slows?
- [ ] Am I holding because of fundamentals or momentum?
- [ ] Have I already taken some profits on the way up?
- [ ] Do I have other diversification if this crashes?
- [ ] Can I hold this through a 50% decline without panic selling?
- [ ] Am I adding to this position after big gains, or taking profits?

If you answered "no" to any of these, your position sizing is too aggressive.
